# Research Notes: Sōba Hidensho Tradition and Attribution History

Compiled 2026-03-27. Sources gathered via web research, library catalog searches, and comparison of existing English-language publications.

---

## 1. The Core Misattribution

English-language trading literature has, since Steve Nison's *Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques* (1991), attributed the **三猿金泉秘録 (Sanen Kinsen Hiroku)** to **Honma Munehisa (本間宗久)** of Sakata, often under the fabricated hybrid title "The Fountain of Gold — The Three Monkey Record of Money."

**This attribution is incorrect.** The text was written by **Ushida Gonzaburō (牛田権三郎)**, pen name **Jiunsai (慈雲斎)**, an Osaka Dōjima rice trader from Ise province. This is stated on the manuscript itself, in the National Diet Library catalog, in CiNii, and in every Japanese-language reference work that discusses it.

The English conflation merged title elements from Ushida's work — "Three Monkeys" (三猿) and "Gold Spring/Fountain of Gold" (金泉) — with Honma's name, producing a phantom text that never existed as a single document.

## 2. Two Separate Textual Lineages

There are two distinct traditions of Edo-period market texts that have been conflated in English:

### A. The Ushida / Sanen Kinsen Hiroku Lineage

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Title** | 三猿金泉秘録 (Sanen Kinsen Hiroku) |
| **Author** | 牛田権三郎 (Ushida Gonzaburō) / 慈雲斎 (Jiunsai) |
| **Date of composition** | 1755 (Hōreki 5) |
| **Form** | Originally 137 tanka poems on trading wisdom (the Kyoto University recension is a revised prose edition from 1851) |
| **Content** | Macro-cycles (junrai/airai), Middle Star mean-reversion, staged position-building with specific ryō calculations, 60-year weather/harvest cycle, Four Virtues, Grand Diagram |
| **Earliest manuscripts** | Kansai University (undated, copying chain documented to 1846); Kyoto University (copy of 1851 printed edition); NDL (1851 printed edition) |
| **Provenance** | Strong. Multiple independent witnesses, documented copying chains. |

### B. The Honma / Sakata Lineage

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Texts** | 宗久翁秘録 (Sōkyū-ō Hiroku), 相場三昧伝 (Sōba Sanmai-den), 酒田戦術詳解 (Sakata Senjutsu Shōkai) |
| **Attributed to** | 本間宗久 (Honma Munehisa) |
| **Date of composition** | Unknown / Disputed |
| **Form** | Prose trading maxims — 157 sections in the Sōkyū-ō Hiroku |
| **Content** | "Three Methods," ceiling/floor identification, behavioral maxims, patience and discipline rules |
| **Earliest printed edition** | 1910 (Meiji 43) — no Edo-period or early Meiji manuscripts survive |
| **Provenance** | Weak. Japanese Wikipedia: "currently transmitted writings attributed to Sōkyū were compiled after the Meiji era." Hayashi Terutarō, *Teihon Sakata Keisen-hō* (1991), p. 3, flagged this. |

### Key differences:
- The Ushida text has real manuscript provenance; the Honma texts do not
- The Ushida text contains the Middle Star system, position-building calculations, and the Grand Diagram — none of which appear in the Honma texts
- The Honma texts contain the "Three Methods" and 157 sections of behavioral maxims — none of which appear in the Ushida text
- Both traditions were paired as the "two great scriptures of market trading" (相場の二大聖典) in Japanese financial culture

## 3. Honma Munehisa: Historical vs. Legendary

### What is historically verifiable:
- A real rice trader from Sakata, Dewa province
- Third son of the Honma family (本間久四郎光本 household) — **not adopted** (the "born Kato" claim in English sources confuses his wife's maiden name with his own birth name)
- Birth name: Kosaku (古作), common name: Kyūsaku (久作)
- Expelled from the Honma household by his nephew Mitsuoka (本間光丘, 1733–1801) over disagreements about using company capital for speculation
- Tomb at Zuitokuji Temple (随徳寺), Taito Ward, Tokyo
- Death year: 1803 (consistent across all sources)

### Disputed:
- **Birth year:** 1724 (Japanese Wikipedia, Weblio, citing *Sakata Jinmei-roku*) vs. 1718 (NDL authority record, citing *Sōkyū-ō Hiroku* ed. Yamaguchi Eijirō) vs. 1717 (English trading literature, Lee Hyongdo). If he died at age 86 (as stated by Shonai Securities), then 1717/1718 is correct.
  - NDL authority record: [id.ndl.go.jp/auth/ndlna/00320671](https://id.ndl.go.jp/auth/ndlna/00320671)
  - Japanese Wikipedia: [ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/本間宗久](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%9C%AC%E9%96%93%E5%AE%97%E4%B9%85)

### What is legendary / incorrect:
- **"Inventor of candlestick charts"** — Almost certainly false. Steve Nison retracted this in *Beyond Candlesticks* (Wiley, 1994, p. 14): "my research indicates Honma likely did not use candlestick charts. Candlestick charts were probably developed in early Meiji (late 1800s)." Shonai Securities (from Honma's hometown of Sakata) states: "the Sōkyū-ō Hiroku contains no discussion whatsoever of charts." Japanese Wikipedia on candlestick charts (ローソク足チャート) describes the Honma attribution as an "alternative theory" (異説), with candlesticks born in the Meiji 30s (late 1890s).
  - Shonai Securities: [shonaisc.co.jp/about/honmasoukyu/](https://www.shonaisc.co.jp/about/honmasoukyu/)
  - Japanese Wikipedia (candlestick charts): [ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/ローソク足チャート](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E3%83%AD%E3%83%BC%E3%82%BD%E3%82%AF%E8%B6%B3%E3%83%81%E3%83%A3%E3%83%BC%E3%83%88)
  - Daniel Cheung's primary source research (2024): [medium.com/@danvim/deep-dive-into-the-opposing-color-schemes](https://medium.com/@danvim/deep-dive-into-the-opposing-color-schemes-in-asian-vs-western-stock-market-prices-part-1-origin-4e3ccdb27c99)
- **"Author of the Sanen Kinsen Hiroku"** — Incorrect. That text is by Ushida Gonzaburō (see §1 above).
- **"Sakata's Five Methods" (酒田五法)** — Likely systematized after Honma's era, with his name attached retroactively through Meiji-era publishing. The Japanese Wikipedia article on 酒田五法 carries an editorial notice that "reference sources are unclear."
  - [ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/酒田五法](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E9%85%92%E7%94%B0%E4%BA%94%E6%B3%95)
- **"Born Kato, adopted into the Honma family"** — Error in English sources. He was born the third son of the Honma family. His wife was from the Kato family.

## 4. Ushida Gonzaburō: What We Know

- **Also known as:** 慈雲斎 (Jiunsai)
- **Origin:** From Ise province (伊勢)
- **Occupation:** Rice futures trader (相場師) at the Dōjima Rice Exchange, Osaka
- **Active period:** Mid-18th century; approximately 6 years older than Honma Munehisa (b. ~1718)
- **Claimed experience:** 60 years of rice trading
- **Birth/death dates:** Unknown; no precise dates documented anywhere
- **Biographical records:** None survive beyond what the text's colophons state. Described by Yahoo News Japan as "a trader shrouded in enigma" (謎に満ちた相場師).
- **Significance:** Recognized in Japanese financial history as one of the "two great figures" of Edo-period market wisdom alongside Honma. Japanese Wikipedia has no dedicated article on him.

## 5. Narukawa Takenosuke (鳴川猛之助)

Virtually nothing is known about this person. He appears only in catalog records as the editor/corrector (校正者) of the 1851 Kōmondō edition of the Sanen Kinsen Hiroku. The NDL catalog describes the text as Ushida's "posthumous manuscript" (遺稿) edited by Narukawa. No biographical information exists online.

## 6. Steve Nison and the Propagation of Error

Steve Nison's *Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques* (1991) brought Japanese charting to the West and became a bestseller. In it, Nison consolidated several distinct figures and texts into a single narrative centered on Honma Munehisa. This involved:

1. Attributing the Sanen Kinsen Hiroku to Honma (incorrect — it's Ushida's)
2. Crediting Honma with inventing candlestick charts (retracted by Nison himself in 1994)
3. Creating the "Fountain of Gold" brand name in English

**Nison's partial retraction** in *Beyond Candlesticks* (Wiley, 1994, p. 14):
> "my research indicates Honma likely did not use candlestick charts. Candlestick charts were probably developed in early Meiji (late 1800s)."

The first book sold millions; the retraction is a footnote in a less-popular sequel. The original narrative has been repeated uncritically in virtually every English-language trading book, website, and Wikipedia article since.

**Felipe Tudela** (*The Secret Code of Japanese Candlesticks*, Wiley, 2008) also states that Honma "had no knowledge of Japanese candlesticks" and that candlesticks are "an add-on after the fact."

- Author: Lee Hyongdo
- Date: March 2014
- Pages: ~303
- Price: $9.99 (Kindle)
- [Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/God-Trading-Honma-Japanese-Investment-ebook/dp/B00J24NXJK) | [Goodreads (4.31/5, 26 ratings)](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/24351592-the-god-of-trading-honma)

- Part II: "Honma's Fountain of Gold" — appears to conflate the Sōkyū-ō Hiroku and Sanen Kinsen Hiroku
- **No specific source text identified.** Working from modern Japanese secondary sources, not manuscripts.

### Books that discuss Honma but are not translations:

- **Steve Nison, *Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques*** (1991, 2nd ed. 2001). Discusses Honma historically but does not translate his texts. Contains the attribution errors described above.
- **Steve Nison, *Beyond Candlesticks*** (Wiley, 1994). Contains the partial retraction regarding candlestick invention.
- **Felipe Tudela, *The Secret Code of Japanese Candlesticks*** (Wiley, 2008). Discusses the Samni No Den (三位伝) system. Argues Honma's method was about market phases, not candlestick patterns.
- **Gregory L. Morris, *Candlestick Charting Explained*** (McGraw-Hill, 2006). Often cited for Honma attribution but specific sentence crediting Honma with inventing the chart form is reportedly unlocatable when researchers check.

### Key finding:
**No scholarly English translation of either the Sōkyū-ō Hiroku or the Sōba Sanmai-den exists.** A 2011–2012 thread on [Trade2Win](https://www.trade2win.com/threads/munehisa-homma-the-candlestick-chart-inventor-17th-century-looking-for-book.134608/) documented traders searching unsuccessfully. Lee's book was the only option found in 2015.

### The 157-section structure of the Sōkyū-ō Hiroku:
The Aono commentary organizes the sections under three overarching principles (三位伝 / Sanmi no Den):
1. **機を待つに仁** — Waiting for opportunity with benevolence (professional basics)
2. **機に乗ずるに勇** — Seizing opportunity with courage (professional tactics)
3. **気を転ずるに智** — Adapting with wisdom (professional trading techniques)

## 12. The English Wikipedia Problem

The [English Wikipedia article on Honma Munehisa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honma_Munehisa) states he wrote the San-en Kinsen Hiroku in 1755. Its references are: an XS.com trading tutorial, the Apple Books "Fountain of Gold," Morris's *Candlestick Charting Explained*, and Nison's *Beyond Candlesticks*.

The [Talk page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talk:Homma_Munehisa) reveals editorial skepticism:
- Editors note the article relies entirely on technical analysis books, not historical sources
- One editor suspects the story "could be a cute story some analyst made up"
- No contemporary historical sources confirming Honma's extreme wealth or achievements were identified
- The only primary source located is the Kyoto University 19th-century manuscript — which attributes the text to Ushida, not Honma

The [Japanese Wikipedia article on Honma](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%9C%AC%E9%96%93%E5%AE%97%E4%B9%85) is more cautious, stating that the texts attributed to Honma may have been "compiled after the Meiji Restoration" and noting that his status as inventor of candlestick charts is "questionable."

## 13. Sakata's Five Methods (酒田五法)

The five patterns:
1. 三山 (Sanzan) — Three Mountains (triple top)
2. 三川 (Sansen) — Three Rivers (triple bottom)
3. 三空 (Sankū) — Three Gaps
4. 三兵 (Sanpei) — Three Soldiers
5. 三法 (Sanpō) — Three Methods (rest pattern)

The [Japanese Wikipedia article on 酒田五法](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E9%85%92%E7%94%B0%E4%BA%94%E6%B3%95) carries a notice that "reference sources are unclear." The [Shonai Securities article](https://www.shonaisc.co.jp/about/honmasoukyu/) states the attribution to Honma "likely resulted from Meiji-era publications using his name in titles, rather than authentic historical connection."

## 14. The Honma Family of Sakata

The Honma merchant house genealogy (from Japanese Wikipedia and multiple sources):

- **Founder (初代):** Honma Haramitsu (本間原光), established the "Niigata-ya" (新潟屋) in Sakata, 1689.
- **2nd generation head:** Honma Mitsutoshi (本間光寿), eldest son of the founder.
- **Honma Munehisa:** Third son of Honma Hisashirō Mitsumoto (本間久四郎光本). Uncle of the 3rd generation head. Not adopted — born into the family.
- **3rd generation head:** Honma Mitsuoka (本間光丘, 1733–1801). Munehisa's nephew. Expelled Munehisa from the household over disputes about rice speculation with company capital.
  - [ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/本間光丘](https://ja.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E6%9C%AC%E9%96%93%E5%85%89%E4%B8%98)

## 16. Summary: The State of Play

**In Japanese:** The attribution is clear. Ushida wrote the Sanen Kinsen Hiroku. Honma's texts are separate, with questionable provenance. This is not controversial.

**In English:** A 35-year-old error, originating with Nison (1991), has been compounded by Wikipedia, anonymous self-publishers, and double-translated books. The two English "translations" available both translate different texts than what they claim, under wrong titles, attributed to wrong authors, from unidentified sources.

**What exists now:**
- Our translation of the Sanen Kinsen Hiroku: the first English translation of the actual text, from an identified manuscript, with real provenance.
- For the Sōkyū-ō Hiroku: no scholarly English translation. The 1910 Japanese edition is freely digitized at NDL. 157 clearly enumerable sections. Ready for translation.
- For the remaining 8 sōba hidensho texts: no English translations of any kind exist.
