## Chapter 52: On the Winter-to-Spring Market

### 第五十二章　冬より春を通じ相場米相の事

From winter(?) through spring, rice(?) prices generally rise. Winter demand(?) is high and inventories low(?) — hence prices rise. They continue rising through spring as long as demand persists. Buy(?) from winter through spring and sell(?) at the May-June ceiling. This is the normal course. However, in some years the ceiling comes in winter and prices fall in spring. Know(?) these exceptions; they depend on harvest quality.

**(Seed)** The winter-spring market depends greatly on the harvest. In abundant years, the rise is small. In famine years, the rise is great. Assess the year's harvest before committing.

## Chapter 53: On Unwinding in Three Parts After a Market Top

### 第五十三章　天井投落直相三つ解きの事

When the market has topped and begins to fall, one should unwind (*toku*, 解く) one's position in three parts. Sell one-third when you believe the ceiling has been reached. Sell another third when prices begin to fall. Sell the final third when prices have fallen further. By dividing the exit into three parts, even if you misjudge the ceiling, you avoid catastrophic loss.

**(Seed)** This three-part method applies to buying as well. Buy one-third at the presumed bottom, another third as prices begin to rise, and the remainder as the rise confirms.

## Chapter 54: On Evaluating the High Price at a Ceiling

### 第五十四章　天井の高値上評の事

To evaluate(?) a ceiling's high price, compare it to the previous ceiling. If higher than the previous ceiling, momentum is strong. If lower, momentum has weakened. The ceiling's height depends on the year's harvest quality and the strength of crowd sentiment.

Furthermore, a ceiling reached after a long steady rise tends to be high. A ceiling reached after a sharp short rise, though high, tends to fall back quickly.

## Chapter 55: On Carrying Positions Past the June Settlement

### 第五十五章　六月越し達の事

Carrying positions past June (*rokugatsu goshi*) means holding rice through the June settlement month. June is a settlement month with heavy selling, so holding through requires ample capital. After June, prices may continue falling in July and August. Without the strength to endure this decline, one should not attempt the June carry-over. Use this method only in years when the overall trend is upward.

---

## Chapter 56: On Large and Small Rises Within Rising Markets

### 第五十六章　上は相場とても相場とて大小の事

Within rising markets there are both large and small rises. A large rise is driven by the overall trend and can last months or more than a year. A small rise is driven by temporary sentiment and lasts only days or weeks. For large rises, commit boldly. For small rises, take modest profits and exit. To treat both the same is a fundamental error.

The same principle applies to falling markets — there are large and small declines. Distinguishing between them is essential.

## Chapter 57: On Misjudging the Relationship Between Money and Rice

### 第五十七章　錢米判相心違の事

Misjudging(?) the relationship between money (*zeni*, 錢) and rice is common in trading. One judges the money market by the rice market and vice versa. Understanding the relationship between these two markets is essential. They do not always move in concert — sometimes they move in opposite directions. Know this discrepancy and correct your judgment accordingly.

## Chapter 58: On Buying at the Water's Low Point

### 第五十八章　買の水下可買

As water flows to the lowest point, so should one buy at the lowest prices. To buy at high prices is to go against the natural order, like water flowing uphill.

---

## Chapter 59: On There Being Two Types of Extremes

### 第五十九章　極相に二つある事

There are two types of price extremes (*gokusō*, 極相). One is the ceiling extreme, where upward momentum exhausts itself and decline begins. The other is the bottom extreme, where downward momentum exhausts itself and advance begins. Identifying these two extremes is the ultimate secret of the way of the market.

---

## Chapter 60: On Paying Attention to Each Day's Extremes

### 第六十章　毎日の極は氣を付け事

Watch(?) each day's high and low. When daily highs gradually climb higher, it is proof of a rising market. When daily lows gradually sink lower, it is proof of a falling market. Keep a record (*chōmen*, 帳面) of daily highs and lows — the overall trend will become clear. To trade without a record is like walking a road blindfolded.

---

## Chapter 61: On Three Consecutive Wins with Big Money

### 第六十一章　大入金三つ勝勢の事

When one commits large capital and wins three times, the overall trend is strong in one's favor. In such a case, continue holding. But after three wins, exercise restraint — arrogance after victory is the seed of defeat. Rest once after three wins and refresh your mind.

## Chapters 62-66: Brief Trading Principles

**Chapter 62** discusses holding from the bottom through the ceiling and the discipline of patience and decisiveness required.

**Chapter 63** teaches that a falling market is the starting point of the next rising market — he who buys at the bottom of the decline reaps the profits of the coming rise.

**Chapter 64** discusses rice prices following natural principles of weather and harvest — human speculation may move prices temporarily, but ultimately nature prevails.

**Chapter 65** teaches that when rice reaches its extreme price, one should abandon the position. To hold past the extreme turns profit to loss.

**Chapter 66** reminds that bought rice must eventually be sold — decide at the time of purchase at what price you will sell. To buy without a predetermined exit invites confusion.

---

## Chapters 67-73: Final Principles of the Upper Volume

**Chapter 67** teaches reading the balance of buying and selling power within a single market session.

**Chapter 68** warns against buying in a market that fails to recover and keeps falling through — wait for the major bottom.

**Chapter 69** applies the three pivotal months framework to identifying turning points for upward moves.

**Chapter 70** warns against buying large quantities of rice at high prices, especially concentrated in a single region. Trade within your means.

**Chapter 71** discusses the relationship between monthly settlement cycles and large buying opportunities.

**Chapter 72** emphasizes that year-long trading (*tōshi sōba*) requires both physical and mental health — rest when fatigued.

**Chapter 73** teaches that when committing large capital, observe three days of price action before moving. Three consecutive days of rise confirm an upward direction; three days of decline confirm downward.

## Lower Volume, Chapter 1: On the Timing of Declines

### 第一章　落節の事

Declines have their own rhythm and timing (*setsu*, 節). Within each month there are one or two regular points of decline. Know these timing points and use them to determine when to buy and sell. The 15th and month-end are common inflection points. Every three months there is a major inflection — January, April, July, and October are the months of major shifts.
