# Pages 59–62: End of the Upper Volume (Ch. 67–73); Beginning of the Lower Volume (Ch. 1–2)

These pages complete the Upper Volume with chapters on trading
without conviction, the futility of following price swings without
a fixed stance, the discipline of the Three-Position Tradition,
the danger of losing sight of the market's overall direction,
pre-November selling caution, range-bound market prudence, and
the secret tradition of waiting two days. The Lower Volume then
opens with a chapter on the margin-call system and the beginning
of a chapter on prolonged consolidation.

The page opens with the final lines of Chapter 66's commentary

**Commentary** (*Shaku*, continued from Chapter 66):

For example, suppose one believes this rice will generally rise
but judges that the advance will be delayed somewhat — that
such-and-such bearish factors remain, and that the price will dip
in the near term. One thinks to sell a little, intending to scoop
up the dip. But this should never be done. Invariably, just when
one expects the decline, the market rises instead. People are
stubborn creatures: when one has committed to a sell position out
of firm conviction that prices must fall, and then the market
rises against that conviction, one finds it all the harder to buy.
Wavering and procrastinating, one falls behind the advance, and
in the end, seized by frustration, one ends up selling in the very
month one should have been buying — and suffers enormous losses.
This calls for the greatest caution. If one expects prices to
rise next month, one should simply wait for a buying opportunity
on the dip.

### Chapter 67: 一分の存念にて賣買無用の事 — *Trading on Personal Notion Alone Is Useless*

> 一　我一分の了簡にて、賣買决して致し間敷也。三位の傳を表にし、立羽を極め、賣買の内立拔く可し。

One must never trade on personal notion alone. Place the
Three-Position Tradition (三位の傳, *sanmi no den*) at the fore,
fix one's stance (立羽, *tateba*), and commit decisively — whether
buying or selling.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

The original text says one must not trade on personal notion.
Taken at face value, this sounds as though one must never trade
on one's own judgment, and one feels a strong urge to object. But
the key lies in the three characters "personal notion" (我一分の,
*ware ichibun no*). What Sōkyū warns against is trading carelessly,
without having assessed the position of the ceiling and floor,
and without having settled on a buying stance or a selling stance.
His meaning is this: combine the Heavenly Stems (十干, *jikkan*)
of the months, consult the charts of monthly and bale-count
position-reckoning to grasp the highs and lows, and only after a
fixed stance — sell if selling, buy if buying — has been firmly
established should one begin to put on a trade.

### Chapter 68: 立羽もなく高下に連れ候事 — *Following the Market's Swings Without a Fixed Stance*

> 一　米の高下に連れ、立端もなく、上く可し下く可しと商致し、五六日も過ぎ、米の動きに連れ、弱氣になり、其節賣過致し、十四五日も過ぎ、又々買氣になり、其度毎に損出る也。是は、商を急ぎ、是非共取る心にて致す故違ふ也。欲を離れ、天井底を見定め、上ならば上、下ならば下と、立羽を極め、三位傳に引合せ、賣なり買なりと立拔く可し。

When one follows the market's swings without a fixed stance,
trading on the thought "it will rise" or "it will fall" — after
five or six days, as rice moves, one turns bearish and over-sells;
after fourteen or fifteen days, one turns bullish again — and
each time, losses accumulate. This happens because one rushes the
trade, determined to capture every move. Abandon greed. Ascertain
the ceiling (天井, *tenjō*) and the floor (底, *soko*). If it is
an up-market, trade up; if a down-market, trade down. Fix one's
stance, check it against the Three-Position Tradition, and commit
decisively to either buying or selling.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

This chapter makes plain the reason why trading without a fixed
stance ends in loss. When one has no settled stance — neither a
buying stance nor a selling stance — one turns seller whenever
the market looks weak, reverses position (*doten*), and when
prices rise a little, reverses again to the buy side. At each
settlement, the result is a loss. This happens chiefly because
one is in a hurry to win. Such trading amounts to forcing a view
where none exists, and loss is inevitable. Therefore, abandon
the desire for gain, do not try to capture every up-swing and
down-swing and range-bound oscillation without exception, but
establish a fixed stance — buy or sell — and devote one's
efforts to capturing the move in one direction.

### Chapter 69: 三位の傳を以て上下鍛練の事 — *Tempering Oneself Through the Three-Position Tradition*

> 一　此商、三位の傳を以て、一體の上下を見極め、二つの内、何程の下げ、何程迄上げ、何程にて止まる。

In this trade, use the Three-Position Tradition to discern the
overall direction of the market. Within a given move, determine
how far the decline extends, how far the advance reaches, and at
what level it halts.

## Page 60 — Upper Volume: Chapters 69 (continued), 70

*(Chapter 69, continued from page 59.)*

> 其節、上方此方作合を考へ、始終如何と丹念致し、假令ば、買に付時は、其間の狂ひ高下に構ひなく、立派を崩さずしつかりとすべき也。思入當る時は、勘定の通り取留る者也。安き處にて買、高き處にて賣るべしと心掛けては、宜しからず、上げと見込む時は、全體を考へ、片買と心得べし。若し、又了簡違ふ時は、早速賣返し休み、得と米の動きを見る可し。其節、米弱みに見ゆる故、賣過するとあり、甚だ宜しからず。是非下ぐべしと存じても賣らざる者也。心得べし。

At that time, consider the crop conditions in the Kamigata and
local regions, attend diligently to how matters develop from start
to finish. For example, when one has taken a buy position, pay no
heed to the erratic swings in between; hold firm and do not
abandon one's stance. When one's assessment proves correct, one
takes profit exactly as calculated. The approach of "buy low, sell
high" may sound sensible, but it is not good practice. When one
judges the market to be rising, consider the whole picture and
commit to buying only (片買, *katagai*). If one's judgment proves
wrong, promptly sell back, withdraw, and observe the movement of
rice carefully. At such times, because rice appears weak, one is
tempted to over-sell — but this is most inadvisable. Even if one
is utterly convinced that prices must fall, one should not sell.
Bear this in mind.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

To observe the market's swings, one must empty the mind and
achieve calm detachment. First, determine whether this is
fundamentally a rising market, a falling market, a range-bound
market (通相塲, *kayoi sōba*), or a consolidation (保合, *mochai*).
Next, in a rising market, how far does it rise and how far does
it pull back? In a falling market, how far does it fall and how
much does it rally? How far does it oscillate, and at what level
does it halt? Investigate the proportions of the swings. Then
factor in crop conditions, stocks of old rice, volume of supply,
the amount of arbitrage rice (乘米, *nosei-mai*) at each location,
the direction of market sentiment (人氣, *ninki*), and so forth.
Consider how, in the end, this market will move.

Once one has established a buying stance or a selling stance
and has begun putting on a position in one direction, one must
press forward with that stance to the very end. One must never
fix one's eye upon the intermediate range-bound oscillations.
Consider the whole of the market and commit to buying only or
selling only (片買又片賣, *katagai mata katauri*). If one's
judgment proves wrong, cut losses promptly, cease trading, rest
and calm the mind, observe the market's course, and only then
resume trading.

Furthermore, when one encounters an intermediate rally or dip,
one is tempted to take a small profit — but until the full
measure of the months and the price level has matured, one
should never take profits.

### Chapter 70: 高下に連れ米の一體を失ふ用心の事 — *Guarding Against Losing Sight of the Market's Overall Direction*

> 一　此書を見極むると雖も、高下に連れ、米の一體を取失ふとある者也。折々、三位の傳に引合せ、全體、米の上か下かを見合せ、月々日々高下を考へ、氣動く時は、毎日賣買に思入附け、年中商致す故、商仕廻の節は詰り損出る也。一年の内、兩度ならでは、丈夫に商致處なき者也。三位の傳を以て考へ、此米、二三ケ月も上るか下るかを得と考へ、賣買共、一ト筋に立拔く可き也。夫共に心元なきとあらば、幾月も相待ち、圖に當る時を考ふ可し。返す返す、三位の傳を離れ、一分の了簡にて商すべからず、肝要のこと也。若し、一分の了簡を以て商致せば、損すると疑なし。

Even one who has thoroughly studied this book will, when carried
along by the market's swings, lose sight of the overall direction
of rice. From time to time, check against the Three-Position
Tradition. Weigh whether rice overall is heading up or down.
When one calculates the highs and lows day after day, month after
month, and whenever the spirit stirs, one attaches a view to
every daily swing and trades all year round — and when the trade
is settled, the result is invariably a loss. In any given year,
there are only two occasions, at most, when one can trade with
real confidence. Use the Three-Position Tradition to consider
whether rice will rise or fall over the next two or three months;
then commit single-mindedly to either buying or selling. If even
this feels uncertain, wait for months on end and consider the
moment when the opportunity aligns. I say again and again: never
depart from the Three-Position Tradition and trade on personal
notion alone. This is of the utmost importance. If one trades on
personal notion, loss is beyond doubt.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

However well one has mastered the meaning of each chapter
explained in this book, once one actually opens a position and
is buffeted by the swings, one's habitual discipline vanishes —
one's eye is caught by the movement, and one can no longer see
the larger trend. Therefore, from time to time, one should
practice something like seated meditation (*zazen*): still the
agitation of one's own mind, and based on the price-tracking
charts (足取表, *ashitori-hyō*) — that is, the line charts
(罫線表, *keisen-hyō*) — study the proportions of the swings,
the passage of months and days, the direction and intensity of
market sentiment, whether the market is ripe or unripe, and so
forth. Only when one's conviction in the direction is free of
doubt should one open a position. And, as stated in the two
preceding chapters, such moments of assured opportunity occur
only two or three times a year. This is reiterated here with
the same meaning, as earnest and repeated counsel.

### Chapter 71: 霜月前引上落引の米賣方無用の事 — *Selling Rice That Has Risen Before November with a Margin Call Is Useless*

> 一　霜月前、五六十俵上にて落引の米は、决して賣ると無用。百俵位にて落引の米は、賣方と心得べし。但し、春夏の米にて、乘米多き時は、百俵上にても、算用に背かざるべし。

Before the November contract (霜月限, *shimotsuki-giri*), if rice
has risen only fifty or sixty tawara and triggers a margin call
(落引, *ochibiki*), one should absolutely not sell. If rice has
risen approximately one hundred tawara and triggers a margin call,
one may regard it as a selling opportunity. However, in the case
of spring or summer rice, when arbitrage rice is plentiful, even
a hundred-tawara rise may not contradict the calculation.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

When the November contract begins to rise from its floor, a
hundred-tawara advance is a matter of course; therefore, at a
mere fifty or sixty tawara, one should not take profits. At a
hundred-tawara rise, that is the standard ceiling price, and one
may take profits. However, in a spring or summer market, if the
spread against other regions is fully developed, even a
hundred-tawara rise does not necessarily mark the ceiling — one
should keep this well in mind.

### Chapter 72: 通相塲用心の事 — *Caution in a Range-Bound Market*

> 一　通相塲抔にて四五百兩も賣付け置き、二十俵方も利分付候時、的へ當ると心得賣込むことあり、大に心得違ひ也。通の高下は、一二俵の者に候得ば、十俵二十俵の高下、早く見切り取留る者也。賣買を浦山敷思ふ可からず。

In a range-bound market (通相塲, *kayoi sōba*), one may have sold
four or five hundred ryō worth and find twenty tawara or so of
profit accrued. Thinking one has hit the mark, one piles on more
— but this is a grave misjudgment. The swings in a range-bound
market amount to only ten or twenty tawara; one should take
profits quickly and not regret having let go of the position.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

When one has opened a position on the view that the market is
range-bound and, say, twenty or thirty chō of profit accumulates,
one is tempted by greed to pile on, forgetting the original
assessment. But this is a great error. The typical price range in
a range-bound market is only twenty or thirty chō. When ten or
twenty chō of profit has accrued, one should promptly take profits
(利喰, *rigui*) and close the position. The market opens every
day and one can always trade again; one should not begrudge
letting go of one's position.

### Chapter 73: 賣買共心進み立候節二日相待秘傳の事 — *The Secret Tradition of Waiting Two Days When the Urge to Trade Surges*

> 一　此米是非〳〵上る可し、今日中に買ふべしと進み立候節、二日待つべし。是非〳〵下くべしと賣氣進む時は、是又、二日待つべし。是極意の秘傳也。總べて、天井直段の時に成ては、見計ひ第一也。天井直段出る時は、賣る可しの心專一也。底直段の節は、買ふ可しの心專一也。此心掛忘る可からず。

When one feels compelled that this rice absolutely must rise and
one must buy today — wait two days. When the urge to sell surges
because prices absolutely must fall — again, wait two days. This
is the innermost secret tradition (極意の秘傳, *gokui no hiden*).
In all cases, when the market reaches the ceiling price (天井直段,
*tenjō nedan*), watchful timing comes first. When the ceiling
price appears, concentrate the mind solely on selling. When the
floor price (底直段, *soko nedan*) appears, concentrate the mind
solely on buying. Never forget this discipline.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

In Sun Tzu's chapter on strategic dispositions (*Gunken-hen*),
it is said: an eye that can see the sun and moon is not thereby
called sharp, and an ear that can hear thunder is not thereby
called keen — for any person's eyes and ears can see and hear
such things. Likewise, when the market nears the ceiling, it
looks to everyone as though it will rise; and when it nears the
floor, it looks to everyone as though it will fall. One is
naturally tempted to trade in the direction of popular sentiment.
But at such moments, the causes of the rise or fall have already
been fully expressed in the price — the market stands at the very
point of reversal. Therefore, no matter how intense the urge to
trade, one should refrain for two or three days. Invariably,
the market will move against popular sentiment.

### Chapter 1: 落掛りの事 — *On Margin Calls*

> 一　何十俵買上何俵にて落る也。
>
> 一　何十俵賣下何俵にて落る也。
>
> 右賣買致す時は、相塲高下の節、油斷なく、左の通此書を以て掛引可申事。
>
> 落切に相成候節は、早速同直段にて買入可申筈。尤も繋ぎに成候節は、買入るゝに及ばず候。

When one has bought and the price has risen so many tawara, the
margin call (落掛り, *ochikakari*) is triggered at such-and-such
a level.

When one has sold and the price has fallen so many tawara, the
margin call is triggered at such-and-such a level.

When executing trades, one must be vigilant during periods of
market fluctuation, and manage margin calls (掛引, *kakehiki*)
according to the following rules.

When a full margin call (落切, *ochikiri*) is reached, one should
promptly buy back at the same price level. However, if the
counterparty carries the position (繋ぎ, *tsunagi*), there is no
need to buy.
