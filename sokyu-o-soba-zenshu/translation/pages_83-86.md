# Pages 83–86: Lower Volume, Chapters 51–64

These pages conclude the Chapter 51 commentary and proceed through
a dense sequence of short chapters, many of which are variant texts
(異本, *ihon*) of earlier Upper Volume chapters. Topics include crop
assessment by region, the primacy of the initial entry, profit-taking
discipline, floor-price recognition, the danger of trading on personal
judgment alone, the Heavenly Stems monthly price cycle, auspicious
and inauspicious calendar days, year-block directional years, seventh-
month *kinoe* years, shipper winter buying, November contract profit-
taking, range-bound falling markets, and margin-call awareness in
profit-taking.

## Page 83 — Chapter 51 (commentary concluded); Chapters 52–53 (opening)

**Commentary** (*Shaku*, continued from page 82):

A market that begins with a decline of only ten tawara is an
exceedingly rare occurrence; such a market will, needless to say,
immediately form a floor. Again, rice that begins at a level
twenty-five or twenty-six tawara down will find its floor within
a decline of fifty or sixty tawara. As described above, if the
floor price (底直段, *soko nedan*) emerges in the seventh or eighth
month, the market will gradually rise over the course of two or
three months through the ninth and tenth months, reaching its
ceiling (天井, *tenjō*) around the first month. When rice is in the
process of rising, one should use a hundred-tawara advance as the
benchmark and continue to add to one's long position through a
rise of seventy or eighty tawara. Depending on crop conditions
(作柄, *sakugara*), the advance may reach a hundred and thirty or
forty tawara, or even a hundred and fifty, sixty, or a hundred
and eighty tawara. One should therefore weigh carefully, using
that year's observed crop conditions, the probable magnitude of
the rise and fall. As a general rule, however, one takes a
hundred-tawara advance as the target for ceiling and floor alike.
In most years, this hundred-tawara advance marks the ceiling;
recognize it as the selling opportunity, and sell with the resolve
of one leaping into fire. One will assuredly reap a great profit.

The only position that guarantees victory for the seller is the
terminal ceiling price (行付天井直段, *yukitsuki tenjō nedan*) —
there is nothing else. This ceiling is the price at which market
sentiment (人氣, *ninki*) has reached its utmost extreme — the
so-called overshoot price. After that point, no matter how strong
the bullish factors that may emerge, the price will not break
above it. Therefore, however poor the harvest and however
promising the outlook, one must not hold a position into the
following spring. If one mistakenly adopts a bullish stance, the
result will be catastrophic loss amounting to the ruin of one's
entire fortune. Once the ceiling price has appeared, close out
every position, rest for thirty or forty days, and set out on a
hot-spring cure or a pleasure trip. If one does not know when to
rest, one may achieve a great profit for a time, but will
assuredly end in failure. This is a point the market trader must
guard against above all. The Master's teaching:

*"Rest; from time to time, reassemble one's defenses — this is
essential."*

This dictum speaks precisely to this point.

### Chapter 52: 上方筋當地作合見計らひの事 — *Gauging Crop Conditions in the Kamigata Region and Locally*

> 一九州、其外、當地共、平作の年には深き高下なし、見計らひ肝要也。諸國當地共、四分が、半作前后の年にて、地方人氣立、黒人素人旅客共買進み、亦郷方、札納、夫食當等迄も買取候程の年、何程上るも知れぬ樣になる故、段々買孕み多く、持詰る故、前年十八九俵廿一二俵位天井直段にて、三四月船盛り迄二十二三俵にて下らざる米は、船手も買はざる故、相塲保合、米持退屈し、六月になり、順氣克く作方能き風聞有之、時により人氣惡くなり、特に六月は、黒人素人共に仕廻月故、六月末迄大下げ疑なし。此事、不作年中作以上の年、前年の天井より夏米の引合、買孕、賣明の考肝要也。

In years when Kyūshū, other provinces, and our own region all enjoy
a normal harvest, there are no deep price swings — careful assessment
is essential. In years when the crop across all provinces and locally
falls to four-tenths or to roughly half the normal yield, local
sentiment grows bullish; professional traders (黒人, *kuronin*),
amateurs, and traveling merchants all buy in; even the rural districts,
the rice-certificate deposits, the provisioning allocations, and the
like are bought up. In such years the market seems to know no limit
to how far it can rise. As a result, the accumulation of long
positions (買孕み, *kaiharami*) grows steadily, and holders are
locked in. If, with the prior year's ceiling price at eighteen or
nineteen tawara, or twenty-one or twenty-two tawara, rice stands at
twenty-two or twenty-three tawara through the third and fourth months
when shipping is at its peak and still does not decline, the shippers
will not buy either; the market enters a holding pattern (保合,
*mochai*), and rice holders grow weary. When the sixth month arrives
and favorable weather and reports of a good crop emerge, sentiment
turns bearish. The sixth month in particular is the closing-out month
for both professionals and amateurs, so a major decline through the
end of the sixth month is beyond doubt. In assessing this, the key
considerations are: in a poor-crop year or a year of middling harvest
or better, the relationship between the prior year's ceiling and the
summer rice prices, the extent of long accumulation, and the degree
of forward selling (賣明き, *uriaki* — that is, short sales by
wholesalers against shipping contracts).

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

The general import of this chapter is as follows. In years when crops
throughout Japan and locally are normal or better, there are no large
price swings. In poor-crop years, the accumulation of long positions
is heavy, and when the following year brings favorable conditions,
these positions are liquidated, producing a summer collapse. Here too,
one should weigh the extent of long accumulation and forward selling
(that is, advance short sales by wholesalers against shipping
commitments), and further consult the commentary on Chapter 9 and
Chapter 51 of this text.

### Chapter 53: 米商踏出し大切の事 — *The Initial Entry into the Rice Trade Is of the Utmost Importance*

> 一米商は、踏出し大切なる者也、必らず、賣買共進み急ぐ可からず。急ぐ時は、決して手違出ると心得べし。今日より外に商場なしと進立候時、二三日四五日、米の通ひを得と考へ仕掛くべし。秋冬共、底直段出ざる内は、幾月も見合せ、丈夫なる時、仕掛く可し。

In the rice trade, the initial entry (踏出し, *fumidashi*) is of
the utmost importance. One must never rush into either buying or
selling. When one rushes, one should understand that a misstep will
inevitably follow. When the urge seizes you that today is the only
trading opportunity, wait two or three days — four or five days —
consider well the oscillation of the rice market, and only then
enter the trade. In both autumn and winter, until the floor price
has emerged, wait for months on end; when the situation is solid,
enter the trade.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

This chapter is a variant text of Upper Volume, Chapter 1. For its
meaning, one should read the commentary on Chapter 1 and grasp
the teaching there.

### Chapter 54: 商利運の時心得の事 — *Conduct When a Trade Turns Profitable*

> 一商利運に向ふ時、勝に乘るべからず。百俵上げ近き時は、唯無難に取留る事を工夫す可し。必らず強慾を思はず、無難に手取して、商仕舞、休むと第一也。

When a trade turns profitable (利運, *riun*), one must not ride the
winning streak. When the advance nears a hundred tawara, devote
one's ingenuity solely to securing the profit safely. Never give way
to greed; take profit cleanly, close the trade, and rest — this is
the first principle.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

The meaning is clear without need of commentary.

### Chapter 55: 底直段を見極むる事 — *Discerning the Floor Price*

> 一底直段を見極め買出す時は、其間の高下に迷ふとなく、立派を極め、自俵上前迄、片買と心得べし。

When one has discerned the floor price and begun buying, one should
not waver at intermediate fluctuations. Hold firm with composure,
and maintain a one-sided buying stance (片買, *katakai*) until the
price has risen a hundred tawara.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

When one has bought at the floor price, one should not be distracted
by intermediate dips in the oscillation, but maintain an exclusively
bullish stance until the price has risen approximately a hundred
tawara. As for identifying the ceiling, the instruction to use a
hundred-tawara advance as the target has been repeatedly stated in
the preceding chapters. See also Upper Volume, Chapter 34.

### Chapter 56: 我一分の了簡立て間敷事 — *One Must Not Trade on Personal Judgment Alone*

> 一我一分の了簡にて商す可からず。年中、三位の傳に引合せ、上るか下るかを得と考へ、前廣より、段々見合せ、仕掛け置くべし。
>
> 買方、八分の利なり。
>
> 賣方、二分の利なり。

One must not trade on the basis of one's own judgment alone.
Throughout the year, compare one's view against the Three-Position
Tradition (三位の傳, *sanmi no den*), consider carefully whether the
market will rise or fall, and from well in advance, gradually
observe and position oneself accordingly.

The buyer's advantage: eight parts out of ten.

The seller's advantage: two parts out of ten.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

When one trades at one's own discretion, one is apt to lose sight
of the market's current positioning and be deceived by prevailing
sentiment — buying at the ceiling, or selling at the floor. One
should therefore study this text closely, cross-check the number
of months and tawara of rise and fall, and thereafter, with an
empty mind and calm spirit, determine whether the market's overall
direction is upward or downward. Rather than forming a hasty
conviction, observe well in advance, and enter the trade only when
one's own judgment accords with the market. The word "well in
advance" (前廣, *maebiro*) carries the implication of seeing
through to the rise or fall several months hence.

### Chapter 57: 月々干支上げ下げの事 — *Monthly Heavenly Stems and Rising or Falling Markets*

> 一甲、乙、丙、丁、上げとす。
>
> 一戊、己、庚、辛、壬、癸、下げとす。

*Kinoe* (甲), *kinoto* (乙), *hinoe* (丙), *hinoto* (丁) — these
are taken as rising months.

*tsuchinoe* (戊), *tsuchinoto* (己), *kanoe* (庚), *kanoto* (辛),
*mizunoe* (壬), *mizunoto* (癸) — these are taken as falling months.

> 右、十二ヶ月の下、月々の干支なり。丙丁に天井直段出る定なれども、多くは、甲より上ると度々也。年中是に當る節、多少に限らず上げなり。但し、其年に因り、前後天井出るとあり。又、廿年に一度、戊己庚辛へ後るゝともあるなり。

The above are the Heavenly Stems assigned to each of the twelve
months. Though the ceiling price is expected to appear in *hinoe*
or *hinoto* months, in many cases the rise begins from a *kinoe*
month and continues repeatedly. Whenever the year contains such
months, the market rises regardless of the magnitude. However,
depending on the year, the ceiling may appear slightly earlier or
later. Also, once in twenty years, it may be delayed into a *tsuchinoe*,
*tsuchinoto*, *kanoe*, or *kanoto* month.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

This chapter records the method of predicting price movements from
the Heavenly Stems (十干, *jikkan*) of each month. This chapter is
a variant text of Upper Volume, Chapter 11.

### Chapter 58: 八専不成就日の事 — *The Eight-Specialist Days and Inauspicious Days*

> 一天一天上、八専、丑日、不成就日、
>
> 右、米段々上げ、百俵上位に成り、此日に當りて高直段出る時は、是れ年中の行付天井直段なり。但し、此日計りに限り、天井出るとにもあらず。十に八九迄、此日天井出るとなり。極々稀には、唯の日、天井出ることもあり、年々の例は、繰にて可考也。

*Ten'ichi-ten'jō* (天一天上), *hassen* (八専), *ushi-nichi* (丑日),
*fujōju-nichi* (不成就日) —

When rice has been rising steadily and has reached the level of
approximately a hundred-tawara advance, and a high price appears
on one of these days, that is the terminal ceiling price for the
year. It is not, however, the case that the ceiling forms
exclusively on these days. Eight or nine times out of ten, the
ceiling falls on one of these days. In extremely rare cases, the
ceiling may appear on an ordinary day. One should examine the
year-by-year records to verify this.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

This chapter states the empirical observation that ceilings and
floors tend to appear on one or another of these four types of
calendar days. This has already been described in Lower Volume,
Chapter 6 of this text, but it is presented here again on the
basis of a variant text.

### Chapter 59: 三年塞酉の方へ廻る年の事 — *On Years When the Year-Block Turns Westward*

> 一三年塞、酉の方へ廻る年は、米上る也、違なし。但し安廻りの年柄、又は、米札申分出て、御藏米一滞り、品澤山等の節は、右に押さると、考へ用ゆべし。
>
> 右三年裏、西の方へ廻る年、秋冬天井直段、其後正二三月迄三四十俵下げ也。持合の時、正二三月甲の月に当る時は、五六十俵上げ決て有之者也。甲乙に上らず、丙丁に上る也。疑ふ可からず。

In years when the year-block (年塞, *toshifusagari*) turns toward
the direction of the Rooster (酉, west), rice will rise — without
fail. However, in years of generally cheap prices, or when rice
certificates have been issued in ample quantity, domain warehouse
rice (御藏米, *okura-mai*) is in arrears, and supplies are abundant,
one should consider that these circumstances will suppress the
advance.

In such years when the three-year block turns westward, the ceiling
price appears in autumn and winter, after which the market declines
thirty or forty tawara through the first, second, and third months.
During consolidation, when the first, second, or third month falls
on a *kinoe* month, a rise of fifty or sixty tawara will assuredly
occur. It does not rise in *kinoe* or *kinoto* months but rises in
*hinoe* or *hinoto* months. This should not be doubted.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

This chapter concerns the guidance for years when the three-year
block turns toward the Rooster, as explained in Lower Volume,
Chapter 5. What is presented here is an excerpt from the variant
text of that chapter. Incidentally, the phrase "suppressed by these
[circumstances]" (右に押さる) means that the market is held down
by the aforementioned conditions and does not rise.

### Chapter 60: 七月甲に當る年の事 — *On Years When the Seventh Month Falls on Kinoe*

> 一七月甲に當る年は、數年の内、上方當地共、夏中決して天災あり、遅き早きは格別、大方急上にある也。是に当る時は、油断すべからず。

In years when the seventh month falls on *kinoe* (甲), within a
span of several years, both the Kamigata region (上方, *Kamigata*)
and our own locality will without fail suffer a natural calamity
during the summer. Whether it comes early or late is beside the
point — as a general matter, the market will rise sharply. When
such a year arrives, one must not let down one's guard.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

This chapter, too, is an abridgement of Upper Volume, Chapter 51,
and what is presented here is its variant text.

### Chapter 61: 船手冬買時の事 — *On the Shippers' Winter Buying Season*

> 一十一、十二、正、右三ケ月は、船手冬買時也。五六十俵上にて落引の米は、決して賣ると無用也。

The eleventh month, twelfth month, and first month — these three
months are the shippers' (船手, *funade*) winter buying season.
When rice on which margin calls (落引, *ochibiki*) apply has risen
only fifty or sixty tawara, selling is entirely futile.

> 百俵上げ位にて落引の米は、賣方と心得べし。
>
> 但し、春夏の米にて、乗米多き時は、百俵上にても、算用に背かざるべし。

When rice subject to margin calls has risen approximately a hundred
tawara, one should recognize it as a selling opportunity.

However, when the rice is spring or summer rice and arbitrage rice
(乗米, *nosei-mai*) is plentiful, even a hundred-tawara advance
may not defy the calculus — that is, there may still be room for

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

These three months are the period when shipper buying flows in
heavily. When this winter buying pours in and drives the market
upward, it can rise as much as a hundred tawara; therefore, one
does not sell at a mere fifty- or sixty-tawara advance. Moreover,
rice in the spring or summer season, when shipping is unrestricted
and rice at various locations stands at a substantial discount, may
still have room for buying even after a hundred-tawara advance.

### Chapter 62: 霜月限商利運の時の事 — *Conduct When a November Contract Trade Turns Profitable*

> 一霜月限の商仕當る時は、心持第一也。譬へば、分限に應じ、千俵利分付かば、五百俵収留むる心になる可し。百俵利分ある時は、五十俵取留る樣心掛く可し。此心持なき時は、慾に迷ひ天井底にて賣買致し、苦勞の上、不利運になる也。此道を心掛る人、常に此心持怠る可からず。

When a trade in the November contract (霜月限, *shimotsuki-giri*)
is going well, one's mental composure is the first priority.
Suppose, in proportion to one's means, a profit of a thousand
tawara has accrued — one should resolve to secure five hundred
tawara of it. When the profit amounts to a hundred tawara, one
should make it one's practice to secure fifty tawara. Without this
mental discipline, one will be led astray by greed, buying at
the ceiling and selling at the floor, and after much toil, the
outcome will be loss. Those who follow this path must never
neglect this discipline.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

This chapter describes the proper approach to profit-taking
(利留, *ridome*) when a trade is profitable. When one judges
that the time has come, one should first close out half one's
position and observe the market's course. See also Upper Volume,
Chapter 59.

### Chapter 64: 買米利運取留心得の事 — *The Discipline of Securing Profits on a Long Position*

> 一買米、利運になり候に付、二三俵方も取留可申と、賣返す事ある也、是は商の道知らざる故也、甚だ不宜也、売返し候とて、少しも安堵ならざる者也。新米の底は、景気付時は商不出急に引上る者故、賣買連れ落になり、手取無之、其上、十一月迄數月の間故甚だ苦勞多し。其上、急に引上る時は其返し又々下落の心遣ひ出る也。賣買手の内にある時は、晝夜無油斷右圖を以て駈引致し、十一月晦日迄油斷なき樣、可心掛事也。

When a long rice position has turned profitable, there are those
who sell back two or three tawara' worth, thinking to lock in a
small profit. This stems from ignorance of the way of trading
and is highly inadvisable. Even having sold back, one gains not
the slightest peace of mind. When new rice forms a floor and
market momentum builds, trading is sparse and the price surges
upward. As a result, both the buy and the sell become subject to
correlated margin calls (連れ落, *tsure-ochi*), leaving no net
gain. Moreover, the position must be carried for several months
until the eleventh month, causing great hardship. On top of this,
when the price surges sharply, one becomes anxious about a
reversal on the sell side. When trades are in hand, one must
maneuver (駈引, *kakehiki*) day and night without complacency,
using the margin-call chart, and remain vigilant through the
last day of the eleventh month.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

This chapter speaks from the trading customs of the Sakata Rice
Exchange (酒田米會所, *Sakata Kome Kaisho*). The point is this:
suppose, for instance, rice is poised to rise ten tawara, and at
a rise of only two or three tawara one sells back in order to
lock in profit. This is a method born of ignorance of the
margin-call (落引, *ochibiki*) rules. Under the customs of the
Sakata Rice Exchange, settlement by forced closing was not
conducted until the eleventh month. Therefore, during the interim,
whether profit-taking or fresh positions, sells are counted as
sells and buys as buys. If the market subsequently rises above
the price at which one took profit, the profit-taking position
too becomes subject to margin calls — producing what is called a
correlated margin call (連れ落, *tsure-ochi*) — and for months
on end one labors with little net gain. Accordingly, one should
consult the margin-call chart in Lower Volume, Chapter 1, and
maneuver without complacency. One would also do well to study the
section on margin calls at the beginning of the volume.
