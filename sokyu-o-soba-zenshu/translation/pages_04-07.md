# Pages 4–7: Biography of Honma Sōkyū; Trading Customs of the Sakata Rice Exchange

## Page 4 — A Brief Biography of Master Honma Sōkyū (beginning)

> 本間宗久翁小傳

### A Brief Biography of Master Honma Sōkyū

> 翁通稱古作、出羽富豪本間氏之族也、本間氏者以大農鳴於海内
> 者也、翁後年卜居江戸根岸、以故世人稱之根岸本間氏云、翁
> 資性敏活、着眼奇警、巧察商界機徴、進退馳驅未曾誤毫厘以故
> 賣買米穀、獲得巨萬利盆、今日本間家得以富冠于全国者。雖因
> 其中興之祖四郎三郎大努増殖、抑亦不翁之輔弼之功不居多焉、
> 今欲叙翁之畧歴、則先不可不溯宗家本間氏致富之所由來也。

The master's common name was Kosaku (古作). He belonged to the
Honma (本間) clan, the great landholding family of Dewa Province,
renowned throughout the realm as foremost farmers. In later years
the master took up residence at Negishi in Edo, and thus the world
knew his line as "the Negishi Honma." By nature quick and spirited,
striking in his perceptions, he possessed an uncanny ability to
read the signs of the market. In his advances and retreats he never
erred by so much as a hair's breadth, and through the buying and
selling of rice he amassed enormous wealth. That the Honma house
today stands as the richest in all Japan is, of course, largely
owing to the great efforts of its restorer Shirōsaburō (四郎三郎) —
yet the master's supporting contribution was by no means small.
To relate the master's career, we must first trace the origins of
the main Honma family's fortune.

> 本間氏本姓佐藤、其先久四郎君、佐渡人也、距今二百五十餘年前
> 落魂來于酒田、爲本間某家僕、某世々住酒田、以海船問屋爲業、
> 稱爲酒田名家、久四郎君資性敏活、忠実盡于其職、大爲主所信用、
> 無幾登用主管、會本間某以事見繋獄、託後事于君、君恐背其委託。
> 勞思勵精、自全舗之監督、至顧客之應接、一以身任之、遂不墜家
> 聲也。已本間氏之寃解歸家、見其資産不減而内外信用如舊也、大
> 喜、謝其勞、與本間氏之稱、設支店而令主之、即今之富豪本間家
> 之始祖也、

The Honma were originally surnamed Satō. Their ancestor, a man
called Kyūshirō (久四郎), was a native of Sado. More than two
hundred and fifty years ago he came to Sakata, destitute, and
entered service as a retainer of a certain Honma household. This
Honma family had lived in Sakata for generations, operating a
shipping agency (*kaisen don'ya*), and was counted among the town's
leading houses. Kyūshirō, quick and diligent by nature, devoted
himself faithfully to his duties and quickly earned his master's
deep trust, before long being elevated to chief steward. It
happened that the head of the Honma house was imprisoned on some
charge, and he entrusted all affairs to Kyūshirō. Fearing above
all to betray this trust, Kyūshirō threw himself into the work —
from overseeing the entire shop to receiving its customers, he
shouldered every responsibility himself — and in the end preserved
the house's reputation undiminished. When Honma's innocence was
established and he returned home, he found his assets undiminished
and his credit, both within and without, intact as before. Greatly
pleased, he rewarded Kyūshirō's service by bestowing upon him the
Honma name and establishing a branch house under his charge. This
was the founding ancestor of the present-day Honma fortune.

## Page 5 — Biography of Honma Sōkyū (continued)

> 庄内之地連年豊饒、米價大賤、久四郎君竊以爲、雖今以田大稔
> 米價大賤也。秋去冬來、有人爭買則米價昂騰可知而已、則擧全力
> 買収正米、果然諸國商人聞庄内米價特賤、爭先來買、是以需要超于
> 供給、米價俄騰、君乘機賣會所買正米、一擧得于金之利矣、君自後
> 連年従事米穀之売買、頻収利益、

The lands of Shōnai (荘内, *Shōnai*) enjoyed bountiful harvests
year after year, and the price of rice was deeply depressed.
Kyūshirō privately reasoned: though the paddies now yield
abundantly and rice is cheap, when autumn passes into winter and
buyers come competing, the price must surely rise. He committed his
entire resources to purchasing spot rice (正米, *shōmai*). Sure
enough, merchants from the various provinces, hearing that Shōnai
rice was especially cheap, came rushing to buy; demand outstripped
supply, and the price leapt upward. Kyūshirō seized the moment to
sell the rice he had purchased at the exchange, and in a single
stroke reaped enormous profit. From then on, year after year, he
engaged in the buying and selling of rice and harvested gain upon
gain.

> 久四郎君沒子某君嗣、早世、其子四郎三郎君承後、四郎三郎號
> 宗善、實爲本間家中興之偉人也。

When Kyūshirō died, his son succeeded him but died young; his
grandson Shirōsaburō (四郎三郎), who took the name Sōzen (宗善),
inherited the house. He was truly the great man who restored and
enlarged the Honma fortune.

> 宗久翁以亨保二年生于出羽國田川郡新堀村、幼爲本間氏二世之
> 猶子、娶其女二世卒、四郎三郎君尚幼、翁以其叔父、權掘家政、
> 居于下藏、

Master Sōkyū was born in the second year of Kyōhō (享保二年, 1717)
in Niibori village, Tagawa district, Dewa Province. As a child he
was adopted by the second generation of the Honma house and married
into the family. When the second head died and Shirōsaburō was
still a child, the master, as the boy's uncle, assumed temporary
charge of the household affairs and took up residence at Shimogura
(下藏) — a corner of Sakata's Funaba-chō where the Honma family's
warehouses stood.

> 翁謂夫欲以商業致富者、不可不擇其販路廣而賣買之圓滑者也、
> 抑兩羽之地、美田沃野亘數十里、古來以産米聞于世矣、加之有
> 最上川廻漕之便、有佳良港灣、運漕之利甚多矣、且酒田有
> 米會所之設、有大圓滑賣買者夫吾所可從事者其米賈也乎、

The master reasoned: anyone who would grow rich through commerce
must choose a trade with broad distribution channels and smooth
transactions. Now the lands of Ryōu (兩羽, the two Dewa provinces)
stretch across tens of *ri* of fine paddies and fertile plains, and
have been known since antiquity as a rice-producing region. Add to
this the convenience of river transport on the Mogami (最上川) and
a fine natural harbor, and the advantages for shipping are
immense. Moreover, Sakata possesses a rice exchange where large and
fluid transactions take place. Surely, then, the trade I should
pursue is the rice trade.

> 從是潜心于米價高底之理、放眼觀察人氣之變遷、數年于茲、
> 一旦豁然有所悟焉、爾後從米穀之賣買、賣則賤、買則騰、莫所
> 爲不有利、忽羸得數萬金

From that time he immersed himself in the principles governing the
rise and fall of rice prices, observing with a broad eye the shifts
in market sentiment (人氣, *ninki*). After several years of this,
one day he experienced a sudden, sweeping enlightenment. From then
on, whenever he traded rice — when he sold, prices fell; when he
bought, they rose — there was nothing he undertook that did not
yield profit. In short order he accumulated tens of thousands of
gold pieces.

> 會四郎三郎君自播州歸酒田、翁擧其利益分附之、携妻遊子大阪、
> 大阪古來以米市場絶全国、所謂米商之本塲者也、翁亦投此、常制
> 機先、其手腕之妙使數世市賈瞠若于後、於是復得巨萬之利矣、

When Shirōsaburō returned to Sakata from Banshū (播州), the master
turned over to him the entirety of his accumulated profits. Then,
taking his wife, he traveled to Osaka (大阪). Osaka had been since
ancient times the foremost rice market in all Japan — the very home
ground of the rice trade. The master entered this arena and
invariably seized the initiative before others could act; the
brilliance of his dealings left traders of many generations gaping
in astonishment. Here again he reaped enormous wealth.

> 還至江戸、方此時翁已富累巨萬、謂米賈非子孫之計、欲仕上野
> 輪王寺宮而未得其途也、會寛水寺鑄大鐘、鐘成甚重、難懸于
> 鐘楼、翁乃建言運米苞山積鐘樓之側、緩勾配、推轉巨鐘、令逹
> 其項、因得不勞而懸之矣、宮聞之、謂警敏可用、召任相摸守、
> 乃搆邸宅于根岸居焉、

He then proceeded to Edo. By this time the master's fortune had
grown immense. Deeming the rice trade no lasting plan for his
descendants, he wished to enter the service of the Rinnō-ji-no-miya
(輪王寺宮) at Ueno but had found no avenue to do so. It happened
that Kan'ei-ji (寛永寺) had cast a great bell, but the bell proved
so heavy that they could not raise it into the bell-tower. The
master proposed a plan: stack bales of rice in a great mound beside
the tower, roll the bell up the gentle slope, and guide it to the
peak — and thus hang it without exertion. The Prince, hearing of
this, declared the man shrewd and useful, and summoned him to
serve with the title of Sagami-no-kami (相摸守). The master then
built a residence at Negishi (根岸) and settled there.

> 翁老譲家于養子猪四郎君、以享和三年八月晦卒于根岸邸、享年
> 八十七。葬于下谷坂本町隨徳寺、法名曰光信院釋宗久、

In old age the master transferred the household to his adopted son
Inoshirō (猪四郎). He died on the last day of the eighth month of
Kyōwa 3 (享和三年, 1803) at his Negishi residence, at the age of
eighty-seven. He was buried at Zuitoku-ji (隨徳寺) in Shitaya
Sakamoto-chō. His posthumous Buddhist name was Kōshin'in Shaku
Sōkyū (光信院釋宗久).

> 翁之逸事不多傳、唯云翁尚在酒田住于下藏也、酒田中町有搗米
> 者善兵衞者、世々爲本間家所眷顧、曾幾暮年買米、失算殆蕩盡
> 家産、尚負債三百金也、

Few anecdotes of the master's life survive. Only this is told:
while the master still lived at Shimogura in Sakata, there was in
Sakata's Naka-machi a rice-pounder named Zenbē (善兵衛) whose
family had long enjoyed the Honma household's patronage. In his
later years Zenbē had speculated in rice, misjudged the market,
and very nearly lost his entire fortune, still owing a debt of
three hundred gold pieces.

> 翁使人候其状、還報曰渠裸體掲米、流汗淋漓、翁聞之以爲此
> 子可助矣、召之問曰、聞汝負債数百金、而日搗米二臼若三臼、
> 安得恢復之乎、善兵答曰、誠如貴言、然奴有妻子在、不可令以
> 一日泣飢寒也、搗米聊資生計耳、若夫恢復之方、則別有所期
> 于後日也、翁曰聞汝買焉而失矣、今若有資金。欲賣買孰執乎、

The master sent a man to observe his situation. The report came
back: he works bare to the waist, pounding rice, drenched in sweat.
The master, hearing this, judged: this man can be helped. He
summoned Zenbē and asked: "I hear you owe several hundred gold
pieces, yet you pound only two or three mortars of rice a day. How
do you expect to recover?" Zenbē answered: "It is as you say, my
lord. Yet I have a wife and children, and I cannot let them go
hungry for even a single day. The rice-pounding merely sustains us.
As for recovery — I have other hopes for the future." The master
said: "I hear that you bought and lost. If you had capital now,
would you buy or sell?"

> 善兵曰奴尚欲買焉、翁曰善、我給汝資矣、唯速買焉、莫顧慮也、
> 善兵衛踊躍、至市大買之、未幾、米價暴騰、遂得恢復資産也、

Zenbē replied: "I would still buy." The master said: "Good. I
shall give you the capital. Buy at once and do not hesitate." Zenbē
leapt for joy, went to market, and bought heavily. Before long the
price of rice surged, and he recovered his fortune entirely.

> 善兵深徳宗久翁、歳次詣根岸謝其恩云、宗久翁曾語善兵衛曰、
> 酒田米賈皆味于見機、我今授汝韜略、乃出書一卷與之、乃此書也、
> 然則此書之價値有不俟言而可知者焉。

Zenbē was deeply grateful to Master Sōkyū and came each year to
Negishi to thank him for his kindness. Sōkyū once said to Zenbē:
"The rice traders of Sakata are all blind to the art of reading the
moment. I now entrust you with my strategy" — and he produced a
single volume and gave it to him. That book is this book. Its value,
then, need hardly be stated.

> 翁生于田川郡一農家、以天稟之敏才與多年之實驗、保其成功、
> 且増殖宗家資産、而其身亦至爲素封家列格式於士流之上、可謂
> 偉矣、翁死既百數十年、而今尚爲米賈所尊重者、豈偶然也乎。

The master was born into a farming household in Tagawa district.
Through innate acuity and many years of practical experience he
secured his success, enlarged the main family's fortune, and raised
himself to the status of a wealthy commoner ranked in standing
above the samurai class. Truly, he may be called great. More than a
hundred years have passed since his death, yet he is still revered
by rice traders. Can this be mere accident?

### Trading Customs of the Sakata Rice Exchange

> 酒田米會所の商習慣

> 酒田米會所の商習慣を述ぶるに當り、先づ酒田の地勢に就て一言
> せざるを得ず。蓋し、酒田の地たる西方一帶は、日本海に面したる
> 港灣にして、最上河の下流に當るを以て、運漕甚だ便宜あり、
> 左れば最上河に副へる沿岸の諸侯は、皆其領内の納米及貢米は
> 勿論、商人の持米に至る迄、大概此港に輸送し来り、米會所の相
> 塲に據て賣買するの慣例にして、最上、米澤、荘内地方の米穀は、
> 皆酒田に集中し来り、無虚数十萬石に上ることあり、

In setting out the trading customs of the Sakata Rice Exchange
(酒田米會所, *Sakata Kome Kaisho*), one must first say a word about
Sakata's geography. Its western expanse faces the Sea of Japan and
lies at the mouth of the Mogami River (最上河), giving it great
advantage for water transport. Accordingly, the various lords whose
domains border the Mogami — their tribute rice and domain-tax rice
as well as merchants' private stocks — all of this was customarily
shipped to this port and traded at the exchange's market. Rice from
the Mogami, Yonezawa (米澤), and Shōnai regions converged on
Sakata in quantities that at times exceeded several hundred
thousand *koku*.

> 又之を購入せんとして、長、雲、石、四、加、能、及松前地方の
> 商買は、各其産物を和船に積載し來りて、商品を販賣して米穀を
> 買取去るを例とす。

Merchants from Nagato, Izumo, Iwami, Shikoku, Kaga, Noto, and the
Matsumae region each loaded their local products onto traditional
sailing vessels (*wasen*), came to sell their goods, and purchased
rice to carry home.

> 左れど、北國地方の常として、毎年冬十月頃より翌春二三月迄の
> 期間は、北風烈しく巨浪怒號して、航海困難なるが爲め、入船
> 少きに拘はらず、出穀多く、隨つて米價も低位に居るを以て、
> 諸州の商賈、冬買と稱して十一二月頃より此地に来りて正米を
> 購入し置き、翌春一番船の上るを待て輸出し去るの常なるを以て、
> 正米の取引甚だ繁昌にして、随つて米取引所の必要を生じ、
> 其歩座方と稱する取引所の如きは、百四五十年以前の創立に
> 係る者なり。

As is common in northern regions, from around the tenth month of
winter through the second and third months of the following spring,
fierce northerly winds and towering waves made navigation
difficult. Despite fewer ships entering port, rice continued to flow
out in volume, keeping the price low. Merchants from the various
provinces therefore came from around the eleventh and twelfth months
in what was called "winter buying" (冬買, *fuyugai*) — purchasing
spot rice and waiting for the first ships of spring to export it.
The spot-rice trade thus flourished enormously, and the need for a
rice exchange arose. The institution called the *Hozakata* (歩座方),
the exchange proper, dates its founding to some hundred and forty or
fifty years before [the time of writing].

> ▲荘内米札　荘内藩の倉荷證書なり、

**Shōnai Rice Certificates** (*Shōnai Komefuda*): warehouse receipts
issued by the Shōnai domain.

> 元和八年酒井忠勝の莊内に封ぜらるゝや、大に農業と開墾を
> 獎勵せるを以て、米穀の産額年を追ふて遞加せり。時に非凡の
> 才能を有せる柴谷武右衛門なる者、家老兼郡代たり、

Tracing their origin: in Genna 8 (元和八年, 1622), when Sakai
Tadakatsu (酒井忠勝) was granted the Shōnai domain, he vigorously
promoted agriculture and land reclamation, and rice production
increased year by year. At that time, one Shibaya Buemon (柴谷武右衛門),
a man of extraordinary ability serving as both chief retainer
(*karō*) and district magistrate (*gundai*) —

> 奮て藩の財政を改革し、酒田鶴岡の兩地に藩廳の倉庫を設置し、
> 毎年十月を期して領内の貢米を此兩倉庫に納入せしめ、之に對して
> 其組の代官兩名づゝの名儀にて、預り米札を發行し、藩士の祿は、
> 凡て此米札を以て給與するの制を定め、

— he vigorously reformed the domain's finances: he established
domain granaries in both Sakata and Tsuruoka (鶴岡), requiring all
tribute rice from within the domain to be delivered to these two
granaries by the tenth month of each year. Against these deposits,
rice certificates (*kome-fuda*) were issued in the names of two
deputies (*daikan*) per administrative unit. Samurai stipends were
all paid in these certificates.

> 且商賈に拂下ぐる御拂米なる者も、又總べて此米札を以てし、
> 倉庫に至り、何時にても正米と引替ふるを得るとゝせり。而して、
> 藩廳に於て其監督を嚴密にすると同時に、隨意に市塲に賣買
> 轉々せしむると、正米と同様に之を許可せるが故に、士民、
> 特に米商の如きは、大なる便宜を得、賣買年を逐ふて昌盛に
> 趣きたり。

Moreover, the rice sold to merchants — so-called "domain-issue
rice" (御拂米, *oharai-mai*) — was likewise all transacted through
these certificates, which could be redeemed at any time for actual
rice at the granary. The domain government supervised the system
strictly while simultaneously permitting the certificates to
circulate freely on the market, treated the same as actual rice.
Samurai and commoners alike, and rice merchants in particular,
found this enormously convenient, and trading grew more vigorous
with each passing year.

**The founding of the Sakata Rice Exchange (歩坐方, *Hozakata*):**

> ▲酒田米會所(歩坐方)の創立は實に源を此米札の賣買に在り

The founding of the Sakata Rice Exchange originated in the trading
of these rice certificates. The certificates possessed several key
advantages: (1) since all domain rice stored in the granaries was
of reliably good quality; (2) the redemption period extended until
the tenth month of the following year, during which time the holder
could exchange the certificate for actual rice at will; (3)
delivery required only the transfer of the certificate itself — the
quality being uniform, there was no need to inspect the rice each
time, nor the trouble of repeatedly measuring, transferring between
warehouses, and the like.

For these reasons, the merchant ships calling at Sakata purchased
certificates through wholesalers (*ton'ya*) in great numbers. Among
the buyers were speculators (思惑人, *omowaku-nin*) who purchased
certificates when prices were low and sold them when the market
rose, profiting on the difference. As this trading grew ever more
active, the various wholesalers agreed among themselves to establish
a common marketplace, dispatching their affiliated brokers
(*nakagai-nin*) each evening to trade certificate rice for set
contract months, with all deliveries settled through rice
certificates. This was the origin of the Sakata Rice Exchange.

**The November Contract** (霜月限, *shimotsuki-giri*):

> ▲霜月限又新商　十一月期のことにして、建米は新米なり發會は
> 大概六月中なり

The "November contract" or "new contract" — this refers to the
November delivery month, in which the contract rice is new-crop
rice. Trading typically opened (*hakkai*) around the middle of the
sixth month.

**Tawara-up and tawara-down** (何俵上げ / 何俵下げ):

> ▲何俵上げ又何俵下げ　藩の制、米の相塲を立つるに、拾兩に付
> 五斗入何俵何分と稱す。故に會所に於ても其唱へに隨ひ、拾兩に
> 付何程と相塲を立て、百兩分を一つ即ち一枚と定む。

Under the domain's system, rice prices were quoted as "so-many
bales (俵, *tawara*) of five-*to* capacity per ten *ryō*." The
exchange followed this convention, quoting prices per ten *ryō*
and defining one "lot" (一枚, *ichi-mai*) as one hundred *ryō*
worth. Thus ten lots equaled one thousand *ryō*, and one hundred
lots equaled ten thousand *ryō*. When the text says "a forty-tawara
rise" or "a hundred-and-eighty tawara rise," it refers to the
change per hundred *ryō*.

**Margin calls** (落引, *ochibiki*):

> ▲落引又は連れ落及緊落引とは、簡短に之を説明すれば、指金
> 落切のことなり。

In brief, a margin call (*ochibiki*), a chain margin call (連れ落,
*tsure-ochi*), and an emergency margin call (緊落引) all refer to
the forfeit-or-extend decision on deposited margin money (指金,
*sashikin*).

In earlier times, rice certificates — equivalent to modern rice
coupons (米券) — served as margin deposits (*shōkokin*). The
threshold for a margin call varied with periodic revisions to the
exchange rules: sometimes twenty tawara of price movement, sometimes
forty. Under the exchange's system — excluding margin calls and
forced liquidations — profit-and-loss settlement could not be
conducted until the contract month arrived. Even trades already
offset (売埋 or 買埋, selling or buying to close) were treated as
separate sell and buy positions until the delivery month; thus, as
prices rose or fell, a margin call against one's sell position or
buy position operated the same as an ordinary new trade.

For example: at a price of twenty-one tawara, broker A buys ten
lots (one hundred *ryō* worth) from broker B, and both deposit
margin money with the exchange. If the price then rises to nineteen
tawara (using the twenty-tawara margin-call threshold), A notifies
B that a margin call has been triggered — calculated at the average
price — and inquires whether B will forfeit the two-tawara margin
and close the position, or deposit additional margin (追敷, *oishiki*)
to carry the position forward. This inquiry is called the "margin
decision" (落斷り, *ochi-danri*).
