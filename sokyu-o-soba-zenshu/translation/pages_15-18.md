# Pages 15–18: Upper Volume, Chapters 1–2

These pages open the main body of the text. Chapter 1 establishes
the foundational principle of Sōkyū's market philosophy: the
paramount importance of the initial entry into a trade.

### Chapter 1: 米商は踏出大切の事 — *The Initial Entry into Trading Is of the Utmost Importance*

> 一　米商は、踏出し大切也。踏出し惡き時は、决して手違になる也。
> 商内進み急ぐべからず、急ぐ時は踏出惡しきと同じ。賣買共、
> 今日より外、商塲なしと進み立時、三日待べし、是傳也。得と、
> 米の通ひを考へ、天井底の位を考へ、賣買すべし。是三位の傳也。
> 底直段出ざる内は、幾月も見合、圖に当る時を考へ、売買すべし。

In the rice trade, the initial entry (踏出し, *fumidashi*) is of
the utmost importance. When the entry is poor, one will invariably
come to grief. One must not rush into a trade; to rush is the same
as a poor entry. Whether buying or selling, when the impulse seizes
you that today is the only chance, wait three days. This is the
Tradition.

Consider well the oscillation of the rice market, weigh the
position of the ceiling (天井, *tenjō*) and the floor (底, *soko*),
and only then trade. This is the Three-Position Tradition (三位の傳,
*sanmi no den*). Until the floor price has emerged, wait for months
on end — consider the moment when the opportunity aligns, and
then trade.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

> (釋)是宗久翁相塲法の總序であつて、以下に説く所の百數十章
> は、總て此序論を細説したる者と見做してよい。

This is the general preface to Master Sōkyū's market method. The
hundred and several dozen chapters that follow may all be regarded
as detailed elaborations of this opening statement.

The "initial entry" (*fumidashi*) means the moment of committing to
a trade. When one hesitates — shall I sell, or shall I buy? — and
vacillates between the two, one enters the trade like a general
whose strategic calculations are already deficient before the battle
begins. Such a trade clearly belongs to the category of losses.

## Page 16 — Chapter 1 (commentary continued)

**Commentary** (*Shaku*, continued):

The chapter teaches that the initial entry is critical — that the
outcome, whether profit or loss, hinges entirely upon the moment
of commitment. Now, to commit to a trade is to begin by fixing
one's assessment, and to fix this assessment requires above all
that one empty one's mind and calm one's spirit. It is for this
reason that Master Sōkyū built a Zen meditation room behind his
house and entered a state of contemplative absorption (*sanmai*).
Yet when we market traders attempt to fix our assessment, there
are demons that seek to cloud the mirror of our judgment even
before the assessment itself takes shape. What are these demons?
They are the spirit of haste.

**(1)** The haste to profit — when one borrows money at high
interest and, compelled by the repayment deadline, forces oneself
to find an opportunity where none exists and opens a position.
This trade is, of course, destined for loss.

**(2)** The envy of another's success — when one sees another trader
profiting and rushes in. This is so-called "jealousy buying" or
"jealousy selling," and it too is a great demon that clouds one's
judgment.

**(3)** The fury of repeated failure — when, after a string of
losses, one trades recklessly out of frustration, without
principle or plan. Such madness is best cured by sitting in Zen
meditation and driving out the demon.

**(4)** The conviction that today is the only chance — when one's
heart surges with the certainty that no better opportunity exists.
This is no demon at all; it is perfectly reasonable. And yet —
wait. Experience shows that what we take for a true opportunity
is often an opportunity in disguise, a false one wearing a mask.
Therefore, rather than rushing in, one should wait three days —
even three hours will do. This is the secret of the market that I
have discovered. Suppress the impulse to rush, calm oneself, and
consider: is this a trending market (*nobi sōba*)? A consolidating
market (*mochai sōba*)? Or a range-bound market (*kayoi sōba*)?
Then weigh the crop conditions of recent years, the volume of old
rice, the quality of the wheat harvest, the movements of the major
players, and other external circumstances; determine whether the
current price stands at the ceiling, the floor, or a middle level;
and only when one's conviction rings true within one's breast
should one commit. It is never too late.

Yet most people, in their haste to profit, cannot wait for the
alignment between their own judgment and the market's timing.
They rush to enter, and the losses mount. The essential secret of
the market lies in selling at the ceiling and buying at the floor.
Until the ceiling or floor has appeared, one should wait for months
on end, and only when the moment arrives should one enter.

> 商急ぐ可からずとは、天井直段底直段を見るとなり。踏出し大切
> とは、考の外のと出る者なれば也。天井底を知る時は、利運にして
> 損なきの理也。利運の米は、強欲を思はず、百俵上げを見切り。
> 商仕廻、四五十日休むべし。休むと云ふは、底直段を見るの理也。
> 前後能々考へ。受用すべし。

"One must not rush a trade" means: watch for the ceiling price and
the floor price. "The initial entry is of the utmost importance"
means: what emerges often lies beyond what one expected. When one
knows the ceiling and the floor, one trades profitably and without
loss. When a trade is profitable, resist greed; use a hundred-tawara
rise as the benchmark and take profits. Close the trade and rest for
forty or fifty days. "To rest" means: to watch for the floor price.
Consider all sides carefully and put this into practice.

## Page 17 — Chapter 1 concluded; Chapter 2 begins

**Commentary** (*Shaku*, continued):

The ceiling typically rises more than a hundred tawara above the
floor, and the floor falls more than a hundred tawara below the
ceiling. Using this hundred-tawara measure as the standard, one
can gauge whether a ceiling or floor is forming, and trading on
this knowledge will invariably yield profit. In most cases, a
hundred-tawara swing marks the ceiling or floor; when one sees the
signs, one should promptly take profit. After closing the trade,
withdraw and rest quietly for forty or fifty days, observing the
market's course at leisure, and wait for the next ceiling or floor
to appear.

This instruction to rest for forty or fifty days after closing a
trade is indeed golden counsel. One can ride success and increase
one's positions, but to take profit and then *rest* — this is what
is truly difficult. Looking at the world's failures, eight or nine
out of ten come from traders who, emboldened by correct forecasts,
pile on more and more positions heedless of the extremity of the
price or the ripeness of the season — buying heavily near the
ceiling, selling heavily near the floor — only to be caught by
the reversal in sentiment and suffer sudden, catastrophic loss.
This is why the master returned again and again to this admonition.

### Chapter 2: 下相塲月頭強く月末弱き事 — *In a Falling Market, the Month Opens Strong and Closes Weak*

> 一　下る米は、月頭は強く、月末廿九日晦日迄下る者也。上相塲
> の通ひは、月頭弱く、月末は強く、急上げの方也。五月迄の賣付は、
> 思入次第廿九日迄見合せ仕廻ふべし。六月の發付は、决して廿一
> 日迄に、サツパリと仕舞ふべし。

In a falling market, rice is strong at the opening of the month and
declines through the twenty-ninth and thirtieth. In a rising
market's range-bound oscillation, the month opens weak and the end
of the month is strong, with a sharp upward thrust. Sell positions
held through the fifth month may be carried, at one's discretion,
through the twenty-ninth before closing. But any sell position in
the sixth month must be closed cleanly (*sappari*) by the
twenty-first, without exception.

**Commentary** (*Shaku*):

This chapter describes the broad pattern of a falling market after
a ceiling has been reached. *Sappari* means to close out completely,
leaving not a single lot.

Sell positions through the fifth month may be carried to month-end
at one's discretion, but the sixth month brings the approach of
the storm season, when prices tend to rally; therefore sell
positions should be closed as early as possible.

## Page 18 — Chapter 2 (commentary concluded)

**Commentary** (*Shaku*, continued):

To explain why the normal pattern of a falling market takes this
form: market sentiment retains the memory of the recent high
ceiling price. The popular feeling of "prices were high" is deeply
imprinted in people's minds, and the new low price inspires a
vague impulse to buy. Particularly in a falling market after a
ceiling has been struck, each month opens at a discount to the
previous close — a pattern that tempts buyers at the month's
opening. Prices briefly advance but, lacking any supporting
developments in the surrounding conditions, the market grows
heavy; doubts arise that the recent high may indeed have been the
ceiling; and from mid-month into the latter part, buying thins
out and bearish sentiment takes hold. Profit-taking by sellers,
together with bull-side liquidation, drives prices still lower.
Yet at month-end, seller profit-taking produces a modest rebound.
In sum, in the aftermath of a ceiling, the typical rhythm is:
month-opening strength, month-end weakness.

The following month opens at a discount reflecting the weak tone
of the prior close, which strikes participants as cheap, making
it hard to sell and tempting to buy on the dip. Any minor bullish
development serves as a pretext for buying — but the market
refuses to follow through, and from mid-month into late month,
bearish sentiment reasserts itself.

There are times when the high comes in the first half of the
month, around the tenth to the twenty-first or twenty-second. But
the normal pattern for a falling market is: month-opening high,
month-end low.

One point requires particular attention, however. After two or
three months of this pattern — month-opening high, month-end
low, month-opening high, month-end low — if at month-end the
expected decline fails to materialize and instead the month closes
*high*, take careful note. Such a market sometimes marks the bottom
— a one-time or annual floor — from which prices promptly break
upward through the monthly range and head higher the following
month. This calls for close attention.
