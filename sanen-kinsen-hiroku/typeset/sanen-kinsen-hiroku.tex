\noindent\textbf{About This Edition}\\[0.3em]
\noindent This is the first English translation of the \textit{Sanen Kinsen
Hiroku}. The manuscript was sourced and the translation project directed
by Zach Booth. The original text is in \textit{kobun} (classical Japanese)
with \textit{kanbun kundoku} reading marks in cursive script
(\textit{kuzushiji}) on aged paper. The translation was produced in two
mechanically-separated stages:\\[0.4em]
\noindent\textit{1. Transcription.} The manuscript images were passed
through \mbox{NDL~\jp{古典籍OCR-Lite}} --- a specialist OCR toolchain
released by Japan's National Diet Library for Edo-period and earlier
classical texts (RTMDet text-region detection + PARSeq character
recognition, ONNX, CPU-only). This step is purely mechanical: the
model reads pixels, not meaning, and cannot silently interpolate from
context it does not have.\\[0.3em]
\noindent\textit{2. Translation.} The OCR output was then rendered
into English with AI assistance (Claude Opus~4.6, Anthropic), working
from the mechanical transcription and consulting the manuscript images
only to sanity-check OCR artifacts.\\[0.5em]
\noindent\textbf{On Confidence.} Section headers, diagram labels,
boxed tables, and structured lists (such as the sexagenary year
cycles, the Three-Virtue verse-counts, and the four-stage method-
diagrams) are legible in the OCR and reliably translated. The dense
prose between these anchors varies: most is readable, but some
stretches --- particularly the afterword on page~32 and several
long interval rows in the body --- are fragmentary even to the
OCR. Where the underlying transcription is difficult, the
translation is marked \textit{\mbox{[Interpretive]}}. Passages so
flagged represent the reconstructed sense of the passage; they are
not the author's exact words.\\[1em]

\noindent\textbf{How to Read This Translation}\\[0.3em]
\noindent The body text of this book is a direct translation of the
source manuscript. The chapter and section headings are editorial,
added for navigation. Footnotes marked ``\textit{Translator's note}''
provide historical context, definitions, and comparative observations.
Indented passages marked ``\textit{Editor's note}'' provide brief
editorial bridges where the manuscript's structure requires
explanation. Chapter lengths vary because the translation follows the
manuscript's own proportions --- some topics receive extensive
treatment in the source, others only a line or two.\\[0.5em]
\noindent\textbf{Structure of the Text.}
Chapter~1 is the preface, grounding the book in Neo-Confucian
cosmology and naming the Three Monkeys as a contrarian discipline.
Chapters 2--3 establish the favorable/adverse year cycle and the
enumerated maxims that act on it. Chapter~4 presents the four-stage
method-diagrams (half-plank, at-ease, reversal). Chapter~5 is the
sixty-year weather almanac. Chapters 6--7 build the decision
framework: the Nine-Body Song and the forty-eight verses of the
Three Virtues. Chapters 8--11 are the quantitative core: the
World-Levels Diagram, the Strong-Qi / Weak-Qi five-stage secrets, the
compound trades, the Grand Diagram, and the three first-tier family
secrets. Chapter~12 is the editor's afterword.

\chapter*{Translator's Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Translator's Introduction}
\markboth{Translator's Introduction}{}

The \textit{Sanen Kinsen Hiroku} (\jp{三猿金泉秘録}), or ``Three Monkeys
Gold Spring Secret Record,'' is one of the most celebrated texts in the
history of Japanese market speculation.\tn{The manuscript is dated
\jp{嘉永四年五月廿日} (1851) and identifies itself as a copy of the
K\=omond\=o (\jp{好問堂}) edition, published at Igarashi-tsu
(\jp{五十嵐津}). The text references the Tenmei era (1781--1789)
extensively for its price data.}

\begin{sloppypar}
The title alludes to the proverb of the three
monkeys---``see no evil, hear no evil, speak no evil''
(\jp{見ざる、聞かざる、言わざる})---reinterpreted
as a warning to the trader: do not be swayed by what you \textit{see} others
doing, what you \textit{hear} as rumor, or what you \textit{say} in haste.
Instead, observe the deeper patterns.
\end{sloppypar}

\section*{Authorship and the Edo Rice Market}

The manuscript names its author as Ushida Gonzabur\=o (\jp{牛田権三郎}),
an Osaka D\=ojima trader from Ise province who wrote under the pen name
Jiunsai (\jp{慈雲斎}). The text was composed around 1755 and later
revised and corrected by Narukawa Takenosuke (\jp{鳴川猛之助}). It
belongs to a genre of ``secret market transmission texts''
(\jp{相場秘伝書}, \textit{s\=oba hidensho}) that circulated among
Edo-period trading houses.\tn{The inner title page of the manuscript
appears to bear a small label that may read \jp{三猿軍書選}
(\textit{Sanen gunsho sen}, ``Selection from the Three Monkeys
Military/Strategy Books''), though the label is partially obscured and
this reading is uncertain.}

During the Tokugawa period (1603--1868), rice was the basis of the economy.
The D\=ojima Rice Exchange (\jp{堂島米会所}) in Osaka functioned as a
sophisticated futures market where ``book rice'' (\jp{帳合米}) was traded
as forward contracts.\tn{The D\=ojima Rice Exchange was established in 1697
as a spot market. Organized futures trading (\jp{帳合米}) was formally
authorized by the shogunate in 1730, making it one of the world's first
organized futures markets.}

\section*{A Note on Attribution}

English-language sources almost universally attribute this text to Honma
Munehisa (\jp{本間宗久}) of Sakata, often under the title ``The Fountain
of Gold---The Three Monkey Record of Money.'' This attribution is
incorrect.

The manuscript names its author as Ushida Gonzabur\=o and its editor as
Narukawa Takenosuke---as do the National Diet Library catalog, the CiNii
bibliographic record, and every Japanese-language reference work that
discusses it. In Japanese scholarship, this is not controversial:
the NDL, CiNii, Kyoto University's digital archive, and sources from
Honma's own hometown (the Shonai Securities company of Sakata) all
attribute the \textit{Sanen Kinsen Hiroku} to Ushida.

Honma Munehisa was a real historical figure---a Sakata trader who was
expelled from his own family for speculating with company capital and who
became legendary in his own right. Texts were later attributed to him
(the \textit{S\=oky\=u-\=o Hiroku} and \textit{S\=oba Sanmai-den}),
though no Edo-period originals of these survive; Japanese scholars suspect
they were compiled in the Meiji era. Honma's texts have entirely different
titles and contents from Ushida's.

The confusion appears to have entered English through Steve Nison's
influential \textit{Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques} (1991),
a book that did enormous service in introducing Japanese technical
analysis to Western traders. In synthesizing a large body of
Japanese-language material for an English-speaking audience, Nison
consolidated several distinct figures and texts into a single
narrative---an understandable simplification given the complexity of the
source tradition. Nison himself corrected the record in
\textit{Beyond Candlesticks} (1994, p.\,14), writing that his research
indicated Honma ``likely did not use candlestick charts.'' But the first
book reached millions of readers, and the broader conflation of names
and titles took on a life of its own.

In the decades since, English sources have merged Ushida's title with
Honma's name, creating a phantom text: ``The Fountain of Gold'' (\jp{金泉})
and ``Three Monkeys'' (\jp{三猿}) are both from Ushida's title; the Honma
texts have completely different names. Two English-language books have been
published under variations of this text's title. Neither translates it.
Both render a separate body of trading maxims from the Honma/Sakata
tradition---a related but distinct lineage---while putting Ushida's title
on the cover.

What follows is, to my knowledge, the first English translation of the
actual \textit{Sanen Kinsen Hiroku}, made directly from the 1851
manuscript held at Kyoto University.

\section*{A Note on Method}

The translation proceeds in two mechanically-separated stages. The
cursive manuscript pages are first read by a specialist optical-
character-recognition toolchain, NDL~\jp{古典籍OCR-Lite}, released
by Japan's National Diet Library for Edo-period and earlier
classical texts. The OCR is mechanical: it reads pixels and emits
characters, with no interpretive access to the text's meaning. An
LLM (Claude Opus~4.6, Anthropic) then works from the mechanical
transcription to produce the English, consulting the manuscript
images only to sanity-check OCR artifacts.

The separation is deliberate. A single model asked to read cursive
script and produce English in one pass has no way to distinguish
between characters it has read and characters it has guessed,
because both are generated by the same mechanism. Splitting the
two stages makes the distinction visible: where the OCR failed,
the book says so; where the OCR returned a plausibly-wrong
character, the correction is flagged inline. Every English
sentence is traceable back to a specific line of mechanical
transcription, which is traceable back to a specific pixel-region
of the manuscript scan.

The full audit trail --- the manuscript scans, the mechanical
transcription at the character level, and the translation files
--- is published alongside this book at the URL given in the back
matter.

\chapter{The Preface: Cosmology of the Market}

\begin{center}
\jp{三猿金泉録序}\\[0.3em]
\textit{Preface to the Three Monkeys Gold Spring Record}
\end{center}

\vspace{1em}

The preface of the \textit{Sanen Kinsen Hiroku} is written in Chinese-
derived prose with Japanese \textit{kundoku} reading marks, in a hand
more formal than the body of the text. Its opening places the rice
market inside the Neo-Confucian cosmology of the Supreme Ultimate and
the alternation of yin and yang, and proceeds through a sequence of
definitions that establish the book's working vocabulary: the strong
and weak forces (\jt{強気}{ky\=oki} / \jt{弱気}{jakki}); the principle
beyond ordinary nature (\jt{天性理外の理}{tensei rigai no ri}); the
empty principle (\jt{空理}{k\=uri}); and the fixed form
(\jt{定式}{j\=oshiki}) by which a method becomes practicable.

\section{The Supreme Ultimate}

\lettrine[lines=2]{T}{he Supreme Ultimate}\tn{\jp{太極}
(\textit{taikyoku}) --- the foundational concept of Neo-Confucian
cosmology. The opening echoes Zhou Dunyi's \textit{Taijitu shuo}
(\textit{Explanation of the Diagram of the Supreme Ultimate},
mid-11th c.), grounding market theory in the highest philosophical
authority available to an Edo-period reader.} moves and begets Yang;
its motion reaches its limit and becomes still. In stillness it
begets Yin; stillness reaches its limit and moves again. Movement
and stillness are the root of one another.

The rise and fall of rice --- like the circuit of Heaven and Earth,
and of Yin and Yang --- alternates between strong-spirited force and
weak-spirited force. Each contains the seed of its opposite: when
the strong force has driven prices to their ceiling, the weak force
is already present within it; when the weak force has driven them to
their floor, the strong force is likewise already present.

\begin{quote}
\textit{When the crowd's spirit is weak, it is the principle of
rice's rising. When the crowd's spirit is strong, it is the seed of
rice's falling. All of this is the principle beyond ordinary nature}
(\jp{天性理外の理}, \textit{tensei rigai no ri}).
\end{quote}

\section{Sixty Years of Study}

The preface turns, at this point, to the autobiographical frame on
which everything that follows rests:

\begin{quote}
\textit{For sixty years I have cast my mind upon this craft,
devising by day and by night, and have at length opened up the
insight into rice's strong and weak forces. I have established the
settled method of rice trading, and have compiled a single volume of
secret writings, giving it the title}~\textbf{Three Monkeys Gold
Spring Record.}
\end{quote}

Six decades of study is a weighty claim. It positions this book as
the summation of a working life's observation, not a younger
trader's prospectus. Every maxim, every diagram, every calibrated
drawdown ladder in the body of the text is offered as what such a
sixty-year span yielded.

\section{The Shape of the Rice Grain}

The preface now turns to the book's central emblem:

\begin{quote}
\textit{The form of the rice grain: rounded in the middle, pointed
at top and bottom. The round is Yang; the pointed is Yin.}
\end{quote}

This single sentence is the seed of the entire diagrammatic tradition
that follows. The grain itself becomes the emblem for the candle-and-
spike pattern of a market print. The same figure will recur in the
\jt{穂足の録}{Hosoku no Roku} (``Record of Spike Patterns'') and in
the four-stage cycle diagrams of Chapter~4.

\section{The Four Classes and the Three Monkeys}

The preface now names the book's broader audience and its guiding
emblem of discipline:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Receiving the breath of Heaven, Earth, Yin and Yang,
\mbox{[rice]} nourishes the four classes --- warrior, farmer,
artisan, merchant --- and is the first treasure of all under
Heaven.}\tn{\jp{士農工商} (\textit{shi-n\=o-k\=o-sh\=o}), the
classical Japanese social stratification. The OCR stream has a
small fragment (\jp{士略}) at this point consistent with the
\jp{士農} opening of the standard list.}
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
\textit{The Three Monkeys are: the See-Monkey, the Hear-Monkey, and
the Speak-Monkey.}
\end{quote}

And then, built on that triad, the book's foundational rule:

\begin{quote}
\textit{When the eye perceives strong-spiritedness, do not let the
heart sink into the abyss of that strength. Hold it only within
the heart, and}~sell.

\textit{When the ear hears weak-spiritedness, do not let the heart
sink into the abyss of that weakness. Hold it only within the
heart, and}~buy.

\textit{Whether you see or hear the strong or the weak, do not
speak of it to others. Words lead hearts astray. This is the secret
of the three monkeys.}
\end{quote}

This is the contrarian discipline that gives the book its title. The
monkeys are not a moralizing ornament but a working rule for trading
against the crowd.

\section{The Empty Principle and the Fixed Form}

The preface closes with a philosophical move that justifies the
enterprise of the whole book: the market's motion is ultimately
empty, but it has a discernible form, and from the form one can
derive a method.

\begin{quote}
\textit{The principle of highs and lows is an} empty principle
(\jp{空理}, \textit{k\=uri}) --- \textit{not visible to the eye; a
thing whose very body is without shade or form.}
\end{quote}

Two readings of ``empty'' are then distinguished. The first: if we
take it only as empty, we will find no hour in a thousand years to
buy or sell. The second: even the formless has a body; where there
is a body, there is a fixed form (\jt{定式}{j\=oshiki}); where there
is a fixed form, there is a trade.

The preface then places the trader's discipline alongside the three
great teachings of its culture:

\begin{quote}
\textit{The fixed form of Buddhism is the Five Precepts. The fixed
form of Confucianism is the Five Constants. The fixed form of Shinto
is the Three Virtues of Wisdom, Benevolence and Courage. Each has
its own fixed form.}
\end{quote}

By naming a ``fixed form'' for rice-trading and then enumerating it
alongside the forms of the three teachings, the preface concludes
with the claim the book will then spend thirty pages demonstrating:
the trader's discipline is not a craft beside the philosophies --- it
is a philosophy in its own right, and its book is the record of its
form.

\chapter{Favorable and Adverse Years}

The preface closes with the reciprocal definition that opens the
body of the text. Of years in which old rice is abundant and its low
price transfers to the new harvest, the book says: \textit{this is
called a} \jt{順来}{junrai} --- \textit{a favorable year}. Of years
in which rice is scarce and the high price transfers onward, even if
a summer rally appears: \textit{this is a year of downward force,
and we call it a} \jt{逢来}{airai} --- \textit{an adverse year}.

\section{The Twelve-to-Thirteen Year Cycle}

The book's opening structural claim is a cycle of twelve to thirteen
years, within which the favorable years typically outnumber the
adverse by roughly ten to three:

\begin{quote}
\textit{The way of following favorable conditions and adverse
conditions --- within a period of twelve or thirteen years, the
favorable years run to roughly ten and the adverse to three.}
\end{quote}

The author presents his method as a table-of-contents in miniature,
naming the eight structural sections that together comprise what he
calls the ``settled form'' (\jt{定式}{j\=oshiki}) of rice trading:
the Twelve-Cycle Trading of favorable and adverse years; the Family-
Tradition Mirror of Highs and Lows; the Thirty-Eight Articles of
Trading; the Fifteen Prohibitions; and the Annual Record of
Fortune-Qi and Plenty-and-Want, among others.

\begin{quote}
\textit{Studied through, this is the wondrous art that does not lose
once in a thousand attempts. Truly, the secret treasure of the
family. Keep it secret, keep it secret.}
\end{quote}

\section{The Attribution}

Immediately following that injunction, the manuscript records a
date and a signature that situates the text in time and lineage:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{\jp{宝暦五年秋九月下旬}} --- \textit{Late ninth month,
autumn of H\=oreki 5} (\textbf{1755}).\\[0.4em]
\textbf{\jp{慈雲斎　牛田権三郎}} --- \textit{Jiunsai, Ushida
Gonzabur\=o}.
\end{quote}

The pen name \jt{慈雲斎}{Jiunsai} appears here, directly above
Ushida's given name. This is the only place in the book where the
pen name is recorded. The date --- \textit{H\=oreki}~5, \textbf{1755}
--- places the text's original composition nearly a century before
the 1851 revised printing recorded in the colophon on the opening
leaf, indicating that Narukawa's edition is the print recension of a
much older family manuscript tradition.

\section{The Twelve-Cycle Trading of Favorable and Adverse Years}

The section header that opens the main body reads:

\begin{quote}
\jp{○順来逢来十二平商内　〔天明本〕}\\[0.3em]
\textit{The Twelve-Cycle Trading of Favorable and Adverse Years ---
Tenmei recension}
\end{quote}

The annotation \jt{天明本}{Tenmei-bon} --- ``the Tenmei-era recension''
--- is one of several running textual-parallel markers that recur
throughout the book, indicating this section descends from a Tenmei-
era (1781--1789) version of the manuscript.

The section is set in verse. Each sub-group contains six verse-lines
per year-type. A representative pair:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Favorable year, autumn-and-winter highs and lows:}

\textit{Autumn cheap; an upward force; one and a half steps; the
eleventh month falls; the year-end rises.}\\[0.3em]
\textit{Spring breaks; the fourth and fifth months go down; the
rice of the sixth and seventh months goes down.}
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
\textit{Favorable year, six verses on the trading method:}

\textit{In autumn, buy; on the upward thrust, sell out and reverse;
turn again on the auspicious month; the year-end is at ease.}\\[0.3em]
\textit{In spring, reverse; in the fourth and fifth months, buy; at
the end of the fifth month, sow this seed; settle by the end of the
sixth, seventh, and eighth months.}
\end{quote}

Each year-type --- favorable and adverse --- receives twelve
verse-lines: six on the expected highs and lows, and six on the
trading method that responds to them.

\section{The Family Tradition: Mirror of the Four Seasons}

A second verse-cycle, more elaborate, is then introduced under the
header:

\begin{quote}
\jp{○家伝四季高安鑑　第二天明本}\\[0.3em]
\textit{Family Tradition: Mirror of the Four Seasons' Highs and
Lows --- Tenmei Recension, Second Section}
\end{quote}

Where the preceding verses addressed the year-type directly, the
\textit{Mirror of the Four Seasons} re-presents the same material
organized by season. The trader is given a paired read for each
season-within-each-year-type --- what to expect in autumn and winter
under a favorable year, what to expect in spring under an adverse
year, and so on --- before the text moves into its first enumerated
maxim-treatise, the Thirty-Eight Articles.

\chapter{The Thirty-Eight Articles\\ and the Fifteen Prohibitions}

The two enumerated sections treated in this chapter form the
backbone of the book's first half. Each is a series of short
maxim-verses (\jt{歌}{uta}), each opening with the enumerative
particle \jp{一} --- ``one.''

\section{The Thirty-Eight Articles of Trading}

The section header reads simply \jt{三十八ヶ条商内}{sanj\=uhachi-kaj\=o
sh\=onai} --- ``Thirty-Eight Articles of Trading'' --- with a small
preliminary ``three articles'' (\jt{三ヶ条}{san-kaj\=o}) grouping at
the head. The furigana gloss \jt{あきない}{akinai} is visible
alongside \jp{商内}, confirming the native (\textit{wakan}) reading
rather than the Sino-Japanese \textit{sh\=onai}.

What follows is a selection of articles grouped thematically. The
full enumeration is preserved in the transcription file.

\subsection{Year-Reads and the Spread Signal}

\begin{quote}
\textit{A favorable year: a downward force followed by a rise. At the
harvest moon of autumn, sow the seed of buying.}\\[0.3em]
\textit{An adverse year: a downward force followed by a further
fall. At the harvest moon of autumn, sow the seed of selling.}
\end{quote}

\begin{quote}
\textit{When the spread (\jp{鞘}, \textit{sayagahari}) flips from
below-spread to above-spread, after the downward thrust comes the
rise --- close out your position on the gain.}\\[0.3em]
\textit{When the spread flips from above to below, after the
downward thrust comes the further decline --- close out your
position on the gain.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{The Two-Hundred-Tenth Day}

\begin{quote}
\textit{The year when strong-spiritedness emerges is set: select it,
and on the} two-hundred-tenth day \textbf{(\jp{二百十日},
\textit{nihyaku-t\=oka})} \textit{sow the seed of buying.}

\textit{A monk says: studying the} Annual Record of Fortune-Qi and
Plenty-and-Want \textit{(the almanac presented in Chapter~5), one
cultivates the strong-spirited stance and, on the two-hundred-tenth
day, sows the seed of buying --- this is the sense of the verse.}
\end{quote}

The two-hundred-tenth day from the start of the lunar year falls in
early September --- the seasonal pivot that recurs throughout the
book as a buying signal.

\subsection{On Public Sentiment}

\begin{quote}
\textit{The ear catching strong-spiritedness in the public voice is
itself the seed of rice's coming fall.}

\textit{When weak-spiritedness has emerged into the open, everyone
turns strong-spirited without judgment --- sow the seed of selling.}

\textit{The year in which weak-spiritedness fails to emerge into the
world is one in which rice ought to rise.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{The Contrarian Maxims}

\begin{quote}
\textit{If a rice whose climbing pace is plainly visible is in
front of you, halt --- stay your strong spirit and sell.}

\textit{The man who is forever weak-spirited, knowing nothing of
loss or gain --- call him a great timid one, a parishioner of the
poverty god.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{The Rhythm of the Year}

\begin{quote}
\textit{The great rule: late in the fifth month is the appointed
time of selling; the harvest moon of autumn is the ripening of
buying.}

\textit{If the year accords with what the multitude expects, better
to set out for an unfrequented path.}

\textit{To buy your rice in a single stroke is rashness. Buy in two;
sell in two.}
\end{quote}

The Thirty-Eight Articles close with two maxims that introduce a
reference-concept carrying forward into the diagrams of Chapter~4:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Across a hundred} tsubo \textit{of land, ninety-nine years
of highs and lows do not exceed three steps --- take this for
fact.}

\textit{Even by a single move, when the step has risen, it is nine-
myriad one} bu\textit{; know it as the selling season at the}
celestial origin \textit{of a thousand.}
\end{quote}

The term \jt{天元}{tengen} --- ``celestial origin'' --- will reappear
as the top stage-marker of every position ladder in the book.

\section{The Fifteen Prohibitions}

The section that follows is the paired negative half of the
Thirty-Eight Articles: fifteen short \textit{don't-do-this}
injunctions, each opening from a market condition and ending with
the phrase \jt{禁制}{kinsei} --- ``trading is forbidden.''

The section header is ambiguous in the manuscript --- \jp{○十五禁言}
at one point, \jp{十五首} at another --- but the received reading is
\jt{十五ヶ条禁制}{j\=ugo-kaj\=o kinsei}, ``the Fifteen Articles of
Prohibition.'' A running red-ink numeral \jp{十四} at the head binds
the section into the larger enumerated count of the book.

\subsection{Representative Prohibitions}

\begin{quote}
\textit{When a trade is without its proper attendant, then whether
or not any gain or loss may be, trading is} forbidden.

\textit{An abundant harvest: the multitude grows weak-spirited; I
too grow weak. Coming into autumn, selling is forbidden.}

\textit{A lean harvest: the minor-spirit is strong, and stronger
again; when rice is plentiful, buying is forbidden.}

\textit{When the rising step is so visible that even the rice
itself seems to say so, though the year is strong, selling in the
fourth month is forbidden.}

\textit{Fifth-month rice --- sentiment weak, yet the price rises.
Until the strength is clearly in, selling is forbidden.}

\textit{On great floods or great winds sending rice spiking upward
--- in any year, buying is forbidden.}
\end{quote}

The structural pattern is consistent across the fifteen: identify a
market condition; identify the direction the crowd will take; name
the direction the trader must \textit{not} take. The prohibitions
are the contrarian discipline of the Three Monkeys worked out in
fifteen specific cases.

\chapter{The Method Diagrams:\\ Half-Plank, At-Ease, Reversal}

The book now shifts from verse and maxim to diagram. The method-
diagrams treated in this chapter are the book's signature
contribution: four-stage sequential cycles in which the trader
executes a specific sequence of buys and sells as the market moves
up and down through a single cycle. Each diagram in the manuscript
is drawn as a four-cell figure with a red-circled numeral ---
\jp{①②③④} --- at each cell marking the order of operations.

\begin{editorialnote}
These are not branching decision trees. Each diagram specifies the
\textit{fixed sequence} of four actions that constitute one complete
cycle. The differences between the three diagrams lie in the
size-relationship between the buy and sell legs --- which in turn
determines whether the cycle leaves the trader net long, net flat,
or net short at its close.
\end{editorialnote}

\section{The Autumn-Moon Eight-Hundred-Ry\=o Ladder}

Before the four-stage diagrams proper, the manuscript presents a
worked scaling ladder on page~10, keyed to the autumn harvest moon
and to the two-hundred-tenth day. The header reads:

\begin{quote}
\jp{大黒天神秋名月商内并二百十日商}\\[0.3em]
\textit{The Daikoku-deity's autumn-harvest-moon trade, together
with the two-hundred-tenth-day trade.}\\[0.4em]
\jp{買桶三平二来半板安楽商内}\\[0.3em]
\textit{Using the buy-bucket, three-flats-two-comings-half-plank-
at-ease method.}
\end{quote}

The ladder builds an 800-\textit{ry\=o} position across six named
stages: \jt{天元}{tengen} (the celestial-origin base reference),
\jt{一乗}{ichij\=o}, \jt{二来}{nirai}, \jt{三平}{sanpei},
\jt{半板}{hanban}, and \jt{安楽}{anraku} (at ease). The three
totals given in plain prose at the head of the ladder are
unambiguous:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Summed: an 800-\textit{ry\=o} buy, seventy}~tawara
\textit{of accumulated rice, approximately thirty}~ry\=o
\textit{in payment, forty}~ry\=o \textit{in deposit --- altogether
seventy}~ry\=o.
\end{quote}

The oral tradition --- \jt{口伝}{kuden} --- declares its governing
use: \textit{``In the harvest-moon trade, one ought to use this
diagram as the square-rule and trade by it.''}

A mirror instruction at the foot of the ladder establishes its
reversibility: \textit{``When reversed, it becomes an 800-\textit{ry\=o}
sell. In the trade before the two-hundred-tenth day, likewise, one
ought to trade by this diagram as the square-rule.''}

A sister ladder on page~11, keyed to the shrines of Nishinomiya
Ebisu and Sumiyoshi Daimy\=oj\=o, runs a 600-\textit{ry\=o} variant
with a 30-\textit{tawara} bucket subtraction, and another runs a
400-\textit{ry\=o} trade with a \jt{転変}{tenpen} reversal at the
pivot. The same stage-names (\textit{tengen}, \textit{ichij\=o},
\textit{nirai}, \textit{sanpei}, \textit{hanban}, \textit{anraku})
recur throughout.

\section{The Half-Plank Diagram}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{diagrams/fig1_tree_positions.png}
\caption*{\small Manuscript page~12: the \textit{half-plank}
(\jp{半板商内之図}, right) and the \textit{at-ease}
(\jp{安楽商内之図}, left) figures, each shown as a four-stage
sequential cycle with red-circled stage-numerals \jp{①②③④}.}
\end{figure}

\begin{center}
\jp{半板商内之図}\\[0.2em]
\textit{Half-Plank Trade Diagram}
\end{center}

Four stages, alternating 1000-\textit{ry\=o} buy and 500-\textit{ry\=o}
sell:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}r l l@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Action} & \textbf{Intent}\\
\midrule
\jp{①}~one   & \jp{千両買} \ \ (1000-\textit{ry\=o} BUY)   & initial position\\
\jp{②}~two   & \jp{五百両売} \ (500-\textit{ry\=o} SELL)   & partial unwind on a rise\\
\jp{③}~three & \jp{千両買} \ \ (1000-\textit{ry\=o} BUY)   & re-enter on pull-back\\
\jp{④}~four  & \jp{五百両売} \ (500-\textit{ry\=o} SELL)   & partial unwind on a rise\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Net across the full four stages: \textbf{2000 bought, 1000 sold ---
1000 held long} at a reduced average cost. The name
\textit{half-plank} (\jp{半板}, \textit{hanban}) refers to the
half-unwind at each up-move: the trader is always running with half
his plank still underfoot.

The explanatory prose continues:

\begin{quote}
\textit{On an autumn day, in a light rain, buy --- and as the spike
runs five-}bu \textit{to one-}koku, \textit{settle out the half.
This is the} half-plank.

\textit{When the next five-}bu \textit{decline arrives, roll back
into a 500-}ry\=o \textit{buy.}
\end{quote}

The method-summary that follows is one of the book's most direct
ethical claims:

\begin{quote}
\textit{There is gain in both rise and fall, without losing the
essence of the rice --- it is the rarest method under Heaven, and
its profit is far beyond the ordinary. Know this.}
\end{quote}

\section{The At-Ease Diagram}

\begin{center}
\jp{安楽商内之図}\\[0.2em]
\textit{At-Ease Trade Diagram}
\end{center}

The at-ease figure is the half-plank's scaled-down, net-flat
analogue. Same four-stage sequential cycle, all legs at the
100-\textit{ry\=o} scale and alternating buy/sell at the same size
so the net position is flat at the end of each complete cycle:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}r l l@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Action} & \textbf{Intent}\\
\midrule
\jp{①}~one   & \jp{百両買} \ (100-\textit{ry\=o} BUY)  & entry\\
\jp{②}~two   & \jp{百両売} \ (100-\textit{ry\=o} SELL) & exit on up-move\\
\jp{③}~three & \jp{百両買} \ (100-\textit{ry\=o} BUY)  & re-enter on pull-back\\
\jp{④}~four  & \jp{百両売} \ (100-\textit{ry\=o} SELL) & exit on up-move\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Net across the cycle: 200 bought, 200 sold --- flat. The method
captures the oscillation between the 5\%-to-one-step bands on
either side of a reference price without carrying any overnight
directional exposure. Hence \textit{at ease} (\jt{安楽}{anraku}).

\begin{quote}
\textit{On a fall-off day of five-}bu\textit{-to-one-}koku,
\textit{sell out without remainder, take the profit, and when the
price declines by another five-}bu\textit{-to-one-}koku, \textit{buy
again as at first; when it rises swiftly, sell out.}
\end{quote}

\section{The Reversal Diagram}

\begin{center}
\jp{転変商内之図}\\[0.2em]
\textit{Reversal Trade Diagram}
\end{center}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{diagrams/fig2_tree_scaling.png}
\caption*{\small Manuscript page~13: the \textit{reversal}
(\jp{転変商内之図}) figure --- four stages, with the sell-legs
(\jp{②}, \jp{④}) each double the size of the buy-legs.}
\end{figure}

The third of the four-stage cycle figures. Same geometric layout as
the half-plank and at-ease, but with a different size-relationship
between the buy and sell legs: each sell is now \textbf{twice} the
size of each buy.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}r l l@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Action} & \textbf{Intent}\\
\midrule
\jp{①}~one   & \jp{千両買}   \ (1000-\textit{ry\=o} BUY)   & entry\\
\jp{②}~two   & \jp{二千両売} \ (2000-\textit{ry\=o} SELL)  & double-size sell on rise\\
\jp{③}~three & \jp{千両買}   \ (1000-\textit{ry\=o} BUY)   & re-cover on pull-back\\
\jp{④}~four  & \jp{二千両売} \ (2000-\textit{ry\=o} SELL)  & double-size sell on rise\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Net across the cycle: 2000 bought, 4000 sold --- \textbf{2000 held
short} at the end. Where the half-plank keeps the trader long-biased
and the at-ease keeps him flat, the reversal figure flips him from
long to short within a single market cycle. The \jp{②} and \jp{④}
legs each unwind the prior buy and simultaneously open a short of
equal size.

The explanatory prose distinguishes two mirror forms of the method
--- the \jt{買桶転変}{kai-oke tenpen} (buy-bucket reversal) and the
\jt{売桶転変}{uri-oke tenpen} (sell-bucket reversal) --- and frames
both as responses to the market's own tempo:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Without straining against the fundamental nature of the
rice, following the strong-and-weak force of the thing in sequence
--- this is the secret of reversal-trading.}

\textit{The reversal-trade, timed to the rhythm of the saw's teeth
--- step into it and you cool the whole body.}
\end{quote}

\chapter{The Sixty-Year Fortune-Qi Almanac}

The manuscript now opens its empirical core: a year-by-year
categorization of the sixty-year sexagenary (\jt{干支}{kanshi})
cycle by expected weather and harvest conditions. The full section
is called:

\begin{quote}
\jp{万歳運気豊凶録}\\[0.3em]
\textit{The Ten-Thousand-Year Record of Fortune-Qi: Plenty and Want}
\end{quote}

The section's opening line gives the empirical base on which the
whole categorization rests:

\begin{quote}
\jp{二百十三年、二来半板出染転変商内}\\[0.3em]
\textit{Compiled from two hundred thirteen years. The two-stage,
half-plank, carry-over-and-reverse trade.}
\end{quote}

What follows is an enumeration of every year in the sixty-year
sexagenary cycle, grouped into seven weather categories. The counts
sum exactly to sixty --- the editor's red-ink annotation at the
section's close declares this plainly: \textit{\jp{以上、大周六十年
なり} --- ``The above completes the great cycle of sixty years.''}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}r l r@{}}
\toprule
 & \textbf{Category} & \textbf{Years}\\
\midrule
1 & \jp{暴風} \ Violent winds       & 12\\
2 & \jp{洪水} \ Great floods        &  5\\
3 & \jp{寒冷} \ Cold and chill      &  3\\
4 & \jp{霖雨} \ Continuous rains    &  2\\
5 & \jp{極凶} \ Extreme misfortune  &  8\\
6 & \jp{豊作} \ Abundant harvest    & 28\\
7 & \jp{中豊作} \ Middling abundance &  2\\
\midrule
  & \textbf{Total}                  & \textbf{60}\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Each category carries a red-ink margin annotation glossing its
five-element correlation: \jp{暴風} is paired with \textit{wood-qi
in concentrated eruption} (\jp{木気鬱発}); \jp{洪水} with
\textit{greater-yin damp earth} (\jp{太陰濕土}); \jp{霖雨} with
\textit{bright-yang shining metal} and \textit{greater-yang cold
water} (\jp{陽明輝金 / 太陽寒水}); \jp{豊作} with \textit{warm-
transformation} (\jp{温化画}).

\section{The Tide and Wind-Free Almanacs}

The chapter closes with two short practical almanacs that served
the pre-industrial rice trader's single largest external risk ---
shipping interruption.

\subsection{On Great and Small Tides (\jp{大潮小汐の考})}

\begin{quote}
\textit{Know the} small-tide \textit{days to be: the 6th, 7th, 8th;
the 9th; the 10th; the 21st, 22nd; and the 3rd, 4th, 5th.}

\textit{All days other than those listed are great-tide. Three days
of small-tide are the ``great-valley'' days --- this is when the
great winds begin to blow. The wind sets off on the flood tide and
dies at the full tide.}

\textit{The flood-tide each day runs four}~bu \textit{more, and the
ebb runs four}~bu \textit{later.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{The Wind-Free Interval}

\begin{quote}
\textit{From} Kanoe-Saru \textit{for eleven days, and from}
Kanoe-Uma \textit{onward --- in the intervening range --- with}
Hinoto-Ushi \textit{as the central day: over the fifteen days above,
the wind does not blow.}

\textit{From the} Kinoe-Tora \textit{day, for seven days further,
still no wind. Over these fifteen days of no wind, this is when
one puts ships to sea. If by some chance the calm fails, it is an
exception --- know that even such an exception is only one year in
a hundred.}
\end{quote}

\chapter{Weather Signs and the Nine-Body Song}

Page~17 of the manuscript carries an interlude between the year-
almanac of the preceding chapter and the Three-Virtues section of
the next. It begins with a short set of folk-almanac weather-signs
--- the kind of practical knowledge that a trader who depended on
shipping and harvest-reports would have carried in his head --- and
closes with the book's most concentrated piece of theoretical
poetry: the Nine-Body Song.

\section{Weather Signs}

\subsection{On the Entry of the Rainy Season (\jp{入梅三十日})}

\begin{quote}
\textit{Know that the rainy season (\jt{梅}{tsuyu}) enters at the
horse-day beginning the fifth month and opens out at the middle of
the sixth.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{On Rainbows (\jp{虹図})}

\begin{quote}
\textit{A morning rainbow --- know in advance as a morning-rain
sign. The rainbow at sunset, on the other hand, is fair weather.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{The Hassen Twelve Days (\jp{八専十二日})}

The \textit{hassen} is a traditional twelve-day interval of the
sexagenary calendar, running from the \jt{壬子}{Mizunoe-Ne} day
through the \jt{癸亥}{Mizunoto-I} day. The almanac assigns it a
specific rain sign:

\begin{quote}
\textit{If light rain sets in at the beginning of the}~hassen,
\textit{know in advance that it will keep raining until the end.}

\textit{If it begins again after a pause, the twelve days --- in
the saying --- pass in an unceasing return-rain that follows the
boats.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{On the Standing Cloud (\jp{玄雲})}

\begin{quote}
\textit{A standing cloud mingled with red --- know it for wind.
White or black --- know it for rain.}
\end{quote}

\section{The Nine-Body Song (\jp{九躰ノ歌})}

The weather-signs give way to the book's most theoretically
concentrated passage: a verse-cycle in which the rice market is
set against each of the nested Neo-Confucian cosmological bodies in
turn. The section opens under the header:

\begin{quote}
\jp{○一部極意　九躰ノ歌}\\[0.3em]
\textit{Part One: The Secret --- Song of Nine Bodies}
\end{quote}

Each body gets a short verse-lemma.

\subsection{\jp{無極} --- The Limitless (\textit{Muky\=oku})}

\begin{quote}
\textit{The principle of highs and lows is an empty principle ---
invisible to the eye. A thing whose very body is without shadow
and without form.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{\jp{太極} --- The Supreme Ultimate (\textit{Taikyoku})}

\begin{quote}
\textit{The principle-without-principle that dwells even in the
midst of right and wrong --- only the rice-market's window.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{\jp{陰含陽} --- Yin Contains Yang (\textit{Ingan'y\=o})}

\begin{quote}
\textit{When ten-thousand of ten-thousand are all weak-spirited ---
rice contains, within itself, the very principle by which it ought
to rise.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{\jp{陽含陰} --- Yang Contains Yin (\textit{Y\=ogan'in})}

\begin{quote}
\textit{When a thousand of a thousand are all strong-spirited ---
rice contains, within itself, the very principle by which it ought
to plunge.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{\jp{高安大法} --- The Great Law of Highs and Lows}

\begin{quote}
\textit{The great flood falls upon the autumn harvest moon; the
peak at mid-month; the end of the fifth month --- these are the
swayings of high and low.}
\end{quote}

The five rungs together name a single thought: the market is
cosmologically empty (\textit{muky\=oku}), yet unified
(\textit{taikyoku}); each of its two motions contains the seed of
its opposite (\textit{ingan'y\=o} and \textit{y\=ogan'in}); and
the law by which the two manifest in time is keyed to the year's
three seasonal pivots (\textit{h\=on-daih\=o}).

\section{The Four Fixed-Form Rules}

Before the virtues begin, the page presents four short headed
definitions --- each a ``fixed form'' (\jt{定式}{j\=oshiki}) of a
specific relation --- that bridge the Nine-Body Song of the
previous chapter into the enumerated maxims that follow.

\subsection{\jp{天ノ性} --- The Nature of Heaven}

\begin{quote}
\textit{By imperial edict or otherwise --- know in advance that
the division of the fifth month is the time at which natural
profit arises.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{\jp{商定式} --- The Fixed Form of Trading}

\begin{quote}
\textit{By edict or otherwise --- follow the five-}bu \textit{one-
step, and forget the twenty-percent three-step as only silver-
string.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{\jp{平来定式} --- The Fixed Form of the Ordinary Run}

\begin{quote}
\textit{Buying and selling should be balanced at a five-}bu
\textit{swing. Another version reads the same way.}
\end{quote}

\subsection{\jp{売買定式} --- The Fixed Form of Buying and Selling}

\begin{quote}
\textit{Know the buying times as the Dragon-Boat Festival and the
harvest moon, with the seasonal anchor at Minazuki (sixth month)
and Fumizuki (seventh).}
\end{quote}

\section{Wisdom (\jp{智}, \textit{Chi}) --- Ten Verses}

A selection of representative verses. The full ten are preserved
in the transcription file.

\begin{quote}
\textit{Once you have become a favorable-trader, you know --- in a
single stroke, even over ten-thousand}~ry\=o --- \textit{the
secret of the one who lays matters to rest.}

\textit{To wait is benevolence. To face the market is to know the
target of the sell-principle --- know it as the wondrous art of
the half-bridge trade.}

\textit{The celestial-origin trading-force may play out even over
three years. Know this as the prime-trade epigraph of the flag-
master.}

\textit{The echo of the cut-edge of Minazuki rice --- at spring-
field time, or in the evening, is a plunge downward.}

\textit{Fifth-month rice, when sentiment is weak and its station
honored even as it climbs --- know this as the season of spike-
upward trading.}
\end{quote}

\section{Benevolence (\jp{仁}, \textit{Jin}) --- Twenty-Eight Verses}

The largest verse-rank in the book. Its editorial gloss gives the
key: \textit{``Benevolence is the awaiting of the horse-hour; it is
inaka-su --- the pulling-back-to-the-country.''} A selection of the
twenty-eight:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Neither rushing the trade nor answering to impatience ---
to wait until the riding-together of waiting and gaining --- this
too is benevolence.}

\textit{Simply: in the urging-urgent trading-force there is no
profit. Buy the five-}bu \textit{cheap; sell the five-}bu
\textit{high.}

\textit{The more you hurry a trade, the more you lose. Simply
stop; change hands and take another look.}

\textit{Not-rushing-the-buy is the secret of the strong-spirited.
Buy on hearing the pattern emerge.}

\textit{Not-rushing-the-sell is the secret of the weak-spirited. In
all seasons, wait for the ripening and sell.}

\textit{The cheap prices that are not needed in three years or two
--- the big-weight hand is the one that waits without rushing.}

\textit{Even in the principle of decline, if the time has come, it
will not go lower. To rush the buy is a great foolishness.}

\textit{When the force that ought to rise is spent, the place where
it descends of itself --- know that as the nature of heaven.}

\textit{The cheap price of the windless two-hundred-twentieth day
--- take it as the fixed form, receive it with the half-plank, and
go.}

\textit{When a price exists such that the ten-thousand to a man
have given up in weariness --- that is the distant edge of the
previous high-run.}

\textit{Wait for a day of rain and wind and sell rice; gain clear
weather and buy rice.}
\end{quote}

The root maxim of the section closes the twenty-eight:

\begin{quote}
\textit{The root source of the favorable-virtue wisdom, in the end,
is the small progression --- and at that source, too, benevolence
stands in first place.}
\end{quote}

\section{Courage (\jp{勇}, \textit{Y\=u}) --- Ten Verses}

The closing rank of the Three-Virtues sequence.

\begin{quote}
\textit{The principle of the jewel: sell into plenty, buy into
cheapness --- this is the great secret of rice trading.}

\textit{If there is a thirty-percent high-low, do not be startled
--- face it.}\tn{Annotated in the manuscript with the Tenmei-
recension marker \jt{天明本}{Tenmei-bon}.}

\textit{Know it as principle: whenever there is a two-step-to-
three-step high-low, face the rice.}

\textit{At the thirty-percent high-low, profit reverses; to accept
the loss without resistance --- that is the secret.}

\textit{When ten-thousand of ten-thousand are strong-spirited,
become the fool and sell the rice.}\tn{The margin here carries a
second Tenmei-recension gloss in classical Chinese:
\jp{米大崩大変不可驚可買} --- ``Great rice-collapse, great change
--- do not be startled; buy.''}

\textit{When field and mountain alike are all turned out and
weak-spirited, become the dolt and buy the rice.}
\end{quote}

And the final courage verse, which the manuscript pairs with a
\textit{kundoku} return-mark \jp{〈レ〉} inverting its last clause:

\begin{quote}
\jp{三俵鞘直照可〈レ〉逆}\\[0.3em]
\textit{Three-}tawara \textit{spread-price: the reversal rule ---
read and reflect in reverse.}
\end{quote}

\chapter{The World-Levels Diagram\\ and the Five-Stage Secrets}

Page~21 opens the manuscript's most concentrated quantitative
section. Three structures dominate: the \textbf{World-Levels
Diagram}, a ceremonial three-tier figure mapping the full range of
the market; the \textbf{Strong-Qi Five-Stage Secret}, a schedule
for scaling a long into declines; and its mirror, the
\textbf{Weak-Qi Five-Stage Secret}, for scaling a short into
rallies.

\section{The World-Levels Diagram (\jp{世の中三段之図})}

The page opens with two national agricultural reference-figures
that frame the scale at which the whole method operates:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Total rice yield: \textbf{28{,}085{,}482}}~koku.\\[0.3em]
\textit{Total field area: \textbf{947{,}801}}~ch\=o.
\end{quote}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{diagrams/diagram_three_levels.png}
\caption*{\small Manuscript page~21: the \textit{World-Levels
Diagram} (\jp{世の中三段之図}). The three tiers of the figure ---
\jp{上} (upper), \jp{中墨} (middle-ink), \jp{下} (lower) --- frame
the full operating range of the market around a central reference
price.}
\end{figure}

\noindent The glosses for each tier, with the manuscript's weight-
and-price annotations:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}l r r r@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Level} & \textbf{Tawara} & \textbf{Weight / }tawara & \textbf{Price / }tawara\\
\midrule
\jp{●}\ \jp{天井}\ Ceiling & 25 & --- & --- \\
\jp{◯}\ \jp{上}\ Upper     & 35 & \jp{一石四斗}  & \jp{四十五匁七分}\\
\jp{◉}\ \jp{中墨}\ Middle-ink (three-star) & 30 + 1\,bu & \jp{一石二斗}  & \jp{五十三匁一分}\\
\jp{◯}\ \jp{下}\ Lower      & 25 & \jp{一石}      & \jp{五十三匁九分}\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The \jt{中墨}{nakasumi} --- ``middle-ink,'' three-starred in the
original --- is the figure's fulcrum: the reference price against
which all further range-reading is done. A linear ten-tenths
(\jt{十分}{jippun}) scale keyed to this level runs down the page:
each \textit{bu} (tenth) of the year's harvest estimate corresponds
to three additional \textit{tawara} on the price axis, building to
thirty at ten-tenths. The manuscript then annotates a column of
past historical years (\jt{寛永甲子}{Kan'ei Kinoe-Ne} = 1624;
\jt{元禄九丙子}{Genroku 9 Hinoe-Ne} = 1696; \jt{享保}{Ky\=oh\=o}
1716--36; \jt{元文丙辰}{Genbun Hinoe-Tatsu} = 1736) with their
actual out-turns, keyed to this scale as an empirical calibration.

\subsection{Applying the Method}

The rule for using the diagram, in plain prose:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Using the three-level method above, one accumulates the
good-and-bad of each year's world. A year of the upper division
may run on for thirty years; a year of the lower division can run
on for eight or nine.}

\textit{Consider the out-turn of the low ranking. In a middle-
division world, if the year's estimate is seven-tenths, multiply
three times seven --- twenty-one tawara. Take this as the middle-
ink price.}

\textit{For such a price: add six tawara of the thirty-percent
drawdown buffer to get twenty-six tawara as the middle law. At
year-end, this holds without variance; not even one hundredth-of-
a-}monme \textit{off.}
\end{quote}

\section{The Strong-Qi Five-Stage Secret (\jp{強気五ツの秘伝})}

A compact five-stage buy-on-dips schedule --- the heart of the
book's accumulation method. It is read against a base established
at the year's new-rice opening price taken as the middle-ink, and
builds a long position into successive drawdowns:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}l l r@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Drawdown from middle-ink} & \textbf{Ry\=o}\\
\midrule
\jp{第一}\ First   & \jp{一割半さげ}  \ 15\,\% down      & 100\\
\jp{第二}\ Second  & \jp{二割さげ}    \ 20\,\% down      & 200\\
\jp{第三}\ Third   & \jp{二割半さげ}  \ 25\,\% down      & 400\\
\jp{第四}\ Fourth  & \jp{三割さげ}    \ 30\,\% down      & 800\\
\jp{第五}\ Fifth   & \jp{三割余}      \ over 30\,\%      & 1{,}000 --- 2{,}000\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{quote}
\textit{Editor's note: These five stages are each a} tengen \textit{trade-
maxim. Whether a thousand or two thousand}~ry\=o, \textit{buy accordingly.}
\end{quote}

The working rule at the first stage establishes the discipline:

\begin{quote}
\textit{First, reckon the year's world at ten-tenths. Even in a
year where the new-rice price is exceptionally high and you are
certain it ought to fall thirty percent --- by no means sell.}

\textit{To buy at the one-and-a-half-tenths drawdown is the great
secret. In any year, to take the new-rice opening price as the
middle-ink --- this is the family secret.}
\end{quote}

The fifth-stage disposition is the book's most aggressive single
instruction:

\begin{quote}
\textit{From a middle-ink of 20 tawara, once the drawdown runs to
three-step-plus and you see 27 or 28 tawara --- buy a thousand or
even two thousand}~ry\=o. \textit{Take it as the one-man's proof-of-
concept. At such a level, the decline is a ``six-and-stream'' event
--- one in a thousand.}
\end{quote}

\section{The Weak-Qi Five-Stage Secret (\jp{弱気五ツの秘伝})}

The mirror schedule: building a short into successive rallies.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}l l r@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Rally from middle-ink} & \textbf{Ry\=o}\\
\midrule
\jp{第一}\ First   & \jp{一刻半あげ}  \ 1.5-step up        & 100\\
\jp{第二}\ Second  & \jp{二刻あげ}    \ 2-step up          & 200\\
\jp{第三}\ Third   & \jp{二刻半あげ}  \ 2.5-step up        & 400\\
\jp{第四}\ Fourth  & \jp{三刻あげ}    \ 3-step up          & 800\\
\jp{第五}\ Fifth   & \jp{三割余}      \ over 30\,\%        & 1{,}000 --- 2{,}000\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The editorial gloss underneath the header reads: \textit{``Same
schedule as above, one and the same person.''} The two five-stage
schedules are meant to be run in tandem by a single trader --- long
into the downside, short into the upside --- with the size-doublings
(100 $\rightarrow$ 200 $\rightarrow$ 400 $\rightarrow$ 800 $\rightarrow$
1{,}000+) meaning that the aggregate stake is concentrated at the
market's extremities, exactly where the trader is most alone against
the crowd.

\chapter{The Compound Trades\\ and the Seven Fortunes}

Pages 25--27 of the manuscript present the book's remaining
composite trading schedules --- the strong-qi / weak-qi reverse-
weight (\jp{逆権}), the favorable-virtue reverse-loss (\jp{順徳
逆損}), the random-edge (\jp{度端}), the stake-use (\jp{立用}), and
the follow-the-color (\jp{随色}) --- grouped under the
chapter-heading:

\begin{quote}
\jp{○七福即生秘密巻目}\\[0.3em]
\textit{The Secret of the Immediate Arising of the Seven Fortunes}
\end{quote}

\begin{editorialnote}
The chapter's name invokes the Seven Fortunes --- the seven deities
of good fortune in Japanese popular religion --- as an emblem of
the multiplicity of the compound methods collected beneath it. What
follows is a small family of trade-schedules, not a numbered list
of seven conditions.
\end{editorialnote}

\section{The Strong-Qi Reverse-Weight Trade (\jp{強気逆権商内})}

\begin{quote}
\textit{The reverse-weight trading force has its middle-ink.
Up through two steps there is no one-step; beyond two steps there
is no five-}bu.
\end{quote}

The trader builds a long across five scaled entries, reaching a
\textbf{1{,}600-\textit{ry\=o}} total at the extreme:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Cumulative: 1{,}600-}ry\=o \textit{long held; 180}~tawara
\textit{drawdown. A ten-percent rally yields 320}~tawara \textit{gain ---
the voice of favorable virtue.}
\end{quote}

\section{Favorable-Virtue Reverse-Loss Variants}

Three further methods, all in the same idiom, keyed to successively
larger rally thresholds:

\begin{description}
\item[\jp{強気一割あげ順徳逆損商内}] Strong-qi ten-percent-up
favorable-virtue reverse-loss. Five-way graduated scale-out
(\jp{五方ならし}) across a rally.

\item[\jp{強気一割半あげ順徳逆損商内}] Same schedule keyed to a
15\,\% rally threshold, in a tighter \textit{tawara} band. Net:
\textbf{800-\textit{ry\=o} sell}, 70-\textit{tawara} drawdown at
the turn, 160-\textit{tawara} profit per one-step decline.

\item[\jp{強気二刻あげ順徳丞様商内}] Same idiom keyed to a two-step
rally. At the \textit{tengen}-mark on the second pass, the position
reverses and a further 200-\textit{ry\=o} sell is initiated.
\end{description}

\section{The Random-Edge Trade (\jp{度端一六の商内})}

\begin{quote}
\textit{From a 20-tawara middle-ink, whenever the market slumps
beyond three steps --- or, alternately, rallies beyond three steps
--- always trade the one-hundred-}ry\=o\textit{-sized lot at the
low level.}

\textit{Even if thinking in terms of one thousand or two thousand}~ry\=o,
\textit{pack it in lightly. Even a thirty-percent-plus drawdown is
only one case in a thousand; even a thirty-percent-plus rally is
only a mountain-of-one in a thousand.}
\end{quote}

\section{The Stake-Use Trade (\jp{立用商内})}

\begin{quote}
\textit{In the stake-use trade, you do not speak of the gap ---
rather, from having heard the future ups and downs of rice, you
reflect carefully and stake accordingly.}

\textit{The stake-use is not much of a wrapping-in for those who
trade in large size --- the reason being that when you hold a long
of 50{,}000 or 100{,}000}~ry\=o, \textit{even if you wish to sell,
as soon as you do, the side-market chases you down six or seven}~bu
\textit{and can pursue you for up to a month.}
\end{quote}

This is the book's most candid acknowledgement of market-impact.
The trader who is large relative to the market's depth cannot
execute at the marginal price --- his own selling moves the market
against him.

\section{The Follow-the-Color Trade (\jp{随色商内})}

The editor marks this method with a warning: \textit{``this
trading-force is the same as the essential trading-equipment''} ---
that is, it is the method a trader must already know how to handle
before attempting. The method itself:

\begin{quote}
\textit{The law of the follow-the-color trade: at those moments
when the genuine awareness is dulled --- when nothing exceptional
has occurred, and one is inclined to say no imperial edict will
come --- rice suddenly rallies five-}bu \textit{or seven-}bu.
\textit{Have that ship sold, and buy at the place where it retraces
five-}bu, \textit{seven-}bu, \textit{seven-}bu. \textit{This is
called} follow-the-color.

\textit{Still, stake only a hundred or two hundred}~ry\=o.
\textit{Without certainty, to overdo the ease is the bold overhand
of luggage.}
\end{quote}

\chapter{The Grand Diagram}

\begin{center}
\jp{第三　上割三下割三之圖}\\[0.3em]
\textit{Third: The Upper-Three, Lower-Three Figure}
\end{center}

The manuscript's most elaborate single figure. It is the third ---

\section{The Secret of the Random Edge (\jp{度端極意の巻})}

Before the diagram proper, the manuscript gives a one-sentence
summary of the whole approach:

\begin{quote}
\textit{The trade called} random-edge \textit{--- cutting desire and
releasing it is its secret. That the greater-than-a-small-gap is
the favorable --- this is the root of rice-trading force.
Therefore, without dispersing once, one cannot hope to rally a
thousand times at the child's seat.}
\end{quote}

\section{Reading the Diagram}

The manuscript's own prose gives the rule for interpreting the
figure:

\begin{quote}
\textit{This diagram --- take it as the secret of the random-edge.
The center, in square-and-circle, figures heaven and earth.}

\textit{East-west and north-south are the four directions; adding
the four corners of}~ken \textit{and}~kon \textit{brings the total
to ten points of discovery.}
\end{quote}

Four points cardinal, four diagonal, plus the center ``heaven-
earth'' box and its ambient field: together these make the
\jt{十分}{jippun} --- ``ten-tenths'' --- scale referenced
throughout the book's range calibrations.

\chapter{Strong Qi, Weak Qi,\\ and the Three First-Tier Secrets}

The manuscript closes its doctrinal chapter with an interpretation
of the Grand Diagram that carries the book's most consequential
empirical claim: the market's normal range is \textbf{not
symmetric}.

\section{The Bullish Bias}

\begin{quote}
\textit{On the buying side: the principles of drought, wind-and-
rain, heavenly disaster, and buying-enclosure --- across spring,
summer, autumn, and winter. Many are the reasons for strong spirit
among those who take these as their ground.}

\textit{On the selling side: there is not one principle other than
waiting for the buying-enclosure to collapse. It is for this reason
that, when the diagram is laid out, there is a one-tenth strength
bias on the buying side.}
\end{quote}

This is the book's load-bearing empirical assertion: the bullish
case has multiple independent triggers, the bearish case has only
one. The structural drift of the rice market is therefore weakly
upward.

\section{The Effect of Strong Qi (\jp{強気の功})}

A monthly weather almanac specifies exactly which bullish triggers
apply in which season:

\begin{quote}
\textit{When wind and rain are violent, there are ship-wrecks in
every season; Edo and Osaka alike have gaps in shipping. The first
principle is that each person takes heed.}
\end{quote}

\begin{description}
\item[3rd--4th month $\rightarrow$ 8th--9th month:] When wind-and-
rain are violent, the effect is flood damage to fields.
\item[4th--5th month:] Drought means no planting. Long rains rot
the barley.
\item[4th--5th month:] A great wind strikes the barley.
\item[6th--7th month:] A great wind strikes the miscellaneous
grains. Drought strikes field crops.
\item[9th month:] A wind strikes the rice directly.
\item[8th--9th month:] Summer rain rots the glutinous rice.
\item[5th--6th month:] Long rains bring cold that can extend half
the year into a damaging autumn.
\end{description}

\section{The Effect of Weak Qi (\jp{弱気の功})}

The mirror case. Where strong-qi triggers rise from agricultural
failure, weak-qi triggers rise from political and social shocks:

\begin{quote}
\textit{At the very ear of a great-high price, there will be
proclamations on sake and the like --- imperial notices. Besides
these, there will also be proclamations of town-inspections and
the like.}

\textit{Or --- a wind that did not blow is said to have blown; a
minor damage is spoken of as though it were the full ten-tenths;
and there are years when feigned buy-ins are performed.}

\textit{Japan is the country of the gods; it is a country that
takes uprightness as its root, and so the natural heaven is present
above it.}
\end{quote}

\section{The Rarity of the Perfect Year}

\begin{quote}
\textit{Without damage at the planting, without the heavenly
disaster of drought or wind-and-rain --- what one may call a world
at the full ten-tenths cannot come to be more than three years in
a hundred.}
\end{quote}

Even in an abundant year, the trader is reminded that Japan is not
one market: \textit{``Even if our own country is abundant, there
may be countries to east, west, south, or north that have been
blown over. Do not, do not, assume from one view of a single
abundant-peace-year that the whole of Japan will be likewise.''}

\section{The Three First-Tier Secrets}

The chapter closes with the three numbered principles that the
manuscript itself names as the first-tier family secrets --- the
method's most condensed opening rules:

\begin{quote}
\textbf{1.} \textit{Every year, read the opening trading-day level
as the} middle-ink --- \textit{this is the first secret.}

\textbf{2.} \textit{Buy the middle-ink at once --- this is the
second.}

\textbf{3.} \textit{At the outset, stake the hundred-}ry\=o
\textit{trade-seal in true gold of one hundred}~ry\=o --- \textit{this
is the third secret.}
\end{quote}

\section{Yin-Rice and Yang-Rice (\jp{陰米陽米})}

A short closing appendix returns to the cosmological vocabulary of
the preface:

\begin{quote}
\textit{There are such things as yin-rice and yang-rice. The rice
that rises is yang; the rice that falls is yin. The reason: yin
rolls into yang; yang climbs directly upward.}
\end{quote}

\section{The Opening Invocation}

\begin{quote}
\textit{In the present age, faced with the needs before me, the
lateness of the work --- I have seen much that is lamented before
it has even been seen --- letters on fine papers, matters now
reduced to paper-scraps.}
\end{quote}

\section{The Central Worry: One Step, a Thousand }Ri

The editor's governing anxiety is stated plainly in the middle
of the text, through a classical proverb:

\begin{quote}
\textit{When the early-spring buying is applied \mbox{[wrongly]}, it
becomes, as the saying has it, a ``one-step error, thousand-}ri
\textit{deviation'' --- and, without helping, reversed, it turns
instead into a great tree-land mistake.}
\end{quote}

The proverb \jp{一歩千里のちがい} --- \textit{a single misplaced
step, a thousand-\textit{ri} deviation} --- is the editorial concern
of the whole project: the book is a secret-transmission text in
which a small error in reading propagates catastrophically into
trading practice.

\section{Why Publish Now}

\begin{quote}
\textit{Over these four or five years \mbox{[of study]}, with the
limited daughter-of-time, the \mbox{[corrected]} manuscripts that
people have been copying and passing on --- many have grown too old
and would be the opposite of right; wishing to pull one out,
compare, and set things right, I have put my hand aside, but the
present-life-edition cannot yet be called settled.}

\textit{And yet, as I say --- ever since I entered upon this path,
the wood-bark years of my own life have gathered, and I still fear
whether I may have, in selecting Ushida Daisuke as the groundwork,
introduced some deviation from his intent.}
\end{quote}

\begin{editorialnote}
The name \jt{牛田大介}{Ushida Daisuke} appears here. Whether
\textit{Daisuke} is a variant or \textit{hon}-name of
\jt{牛田権三郎}{Gonzabur\=o} --- the named author of the printed
colophon --- or a separate figure in the Ushida transmission line,
is not made explicit in the surviving text. The uncertainty is
preserved rather than smoothed: if the two are the same man, this
is the afterword's clearest evidence of that fact; if they are
different, the editor's worry is about his choice of source among
multiple Ushida transmissions.
\end{editorialnote}

\chapter*{Glossary of Key Terms}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Glossary of Key Terms}
\markboth{Glossary of Key Terms}{}

\begin{description}[style=nextline, leftmargin=2em, font=\normalfont\bfseries]

\item[\jp{逢来} (\textit{airai})]
Adverse conditions; a period of generally falling prices, typically
lasting roughly three years within a twelve-to-thirteen-year cycle.

\item[\jp{安楽} (\textit{anraku})]
``At ease''; the second of the four-stage method diagrams
(Chapter~4). A net-flat cycle of four alternating
100-\textit{ry\=o} buys and sells --- so called because the trader
carries no overnight directional exposure.

\item[\jp{智} (\textit{chi})]
Wisdom; the first of the \textit{three} trading virtues
(智仁勇). Seeing the pattern, knowing the season.

\item[\jp{帳合米} (\textit{ch\=oaimai})]
``Book rice''; forward contracts on rice, as opposed to physical
rice.

\item[\jp{中墨} (\textit{nakasumi} / \textit{ch\=uboku})]
``Middle-ink''; the three-starred center-cell of the World-Levels
Diagram (Chapter~8); the reference price against which the
five-stage strong-qi and weak-qi schedules are read. Taken each
year as the new-rice opening price.

\item[\jp{度端} (\textit{dohan})]
``Random-edge''; the family of discretionary scaling trades
presented on page~28 under the heading \jp{度端極意の巻} (``The
Secret of the Random-Edge'').

\item[\jp{堂島} (\textit{D\=ojima})]
The D\=ojima Rice Exchange in Osaka; the central marketplace for
rice futures trading during the Edo period.

\item[\jp{半板} (\textit{hanban})]
``Half-plank''; the first of the four-stage method diagrams
(Chapter~4). A net-long cycle of 1000-\textit{ry\=o} buys
alternating with 500-\textit{ry\=o} sells --- the trader is always
running with half his plank still underfoot.

\item[\jp{穂足} (\textit{hosoku})]
``Spike-foot,'' or the ear-of-grain figure; the foundational
candle-pattern metaphor introduced in the preface, from the shape
of the rice grain itself (round in the middle, pointed at top and
bottom).

\item[\jp{仁} (\textit{jin})]
Benevolence; the second of the three trading virtues, and the
largest of the three verse-ranks (twenty-eight of the forty-eight
verses). Patience, waiting, not rushing.

\item[\jp{人気} (\textit{jinki})]
``People-qi''; market sentiment; the collective mood of traders.

\item[\jp{慈雲斎} (\textit{Jiunsai})]
Ushida Gonzabur\=o's pen name; appears once in the manuscript
immediately above his given name (page~5), attached to the text's
original composition in H\=oreki~5 (1755).

\item[\jp{順来} (\textit{junrai})]
Favorable conditions; a period of generally rising prices,
typically lasting roughly ten years in a twelve-to-thirteen-year
cycle.

\item[\jp{順徳} (\textit{juntoku})]
``Favorable virtue''; the positive-expectancy direction of the
compound reverse-loss trades of Chapter~9.

\item[\jp{禁制} (\textit{kinsei})]
``Prohibition''; the closing clause-word of each of the Fifteen
Prohibitions (Chapter~3).

\item[\jp{古米} (\textit{komai})]
Old rice; rice from previous harvests.

\item[\jp{空理} (\textit{k\=uri})]
``Empty principle''; the preface's term for the inherent
formlessness of market motion. Paired with \jp{定式}
(\textit{j\=oshiki}, ``fixed form'') in the philosophical move that
justifies the whole project: the motion is empty, yet has a form,
and from the form a method can be derived.

\item[\jp{両} (\textit{ry\=o})]
The gold coin unit of the Edo period; the standard unit of capital.

\item[\jp{新米} (\textit{shinmai})]
New rice; freshly harvested rice. The opening trading-day price of
new rice is taken as the \textit{nakasumi} each year.

\item[\jp{底} (\textit{soko})]
Bottom or floor; the lowest point of the price range.

\item[\jp{太極} (\textit{taikyoku})]
Supreme Ultimate; the Neo-Confucian concept representing
undifferentiated unity. Opens the preface; named in the Nine-Body
Song (Chapter~6).

\item[\jp{俵} (\textit{tawara} / \textit{hy\=o})]
A bale of rice; the standard unit of measure and price quotation.

\item[\jp{天元} (\textit{tengen})]
``Celestial origin''; the top stage-marker of every position
ladder in the book --- the baseline price around which
accumulation and distribution ladders are calibrated.

\item[\jp{天井} (\textit{tenj\=o})]
Ceiling; the top of the price range on the World-Levels Diagram.

\item[\jp{転変} (\textit{tenpen})]
``Reversal''; the third of the four-stage method diagrams
(Chapter~4). A net-short cycle of 1000-\textit{ry\=o} buys
alternating with 2000-\textit{ry\=o} sells --- each sell
simultaneously unwinds the prior buy and opens an equal-size short.

\item[\jp{天明本} (\textit{Tenmei-bon})]
``Tenmei recension''; the margin-annotation marker that recurs
throughout the book indicating a parallel reading from a Tenmei-
era (1781--1789) version of the text.

\item[\jp{強気} (\textit{ky\=oki})]
Strong qi; bullish sentiment or upward force. One of the two
central force-concepts of the book.

\item[\jp{運気} (\textit{unki})]
Fortune-qi; the prevailing momentum of a given period. Compiled as
an almanac in Chapter~5, the \jp{万歳運気豊凶録}.

\item[\jp{弱気} (\textit{jakki} / \textit{yowaki})]
Weak qi; bearish sentiment or downward force.

\item[\jp{勇} (\textit{y\=u})]
Courage; the third of the three trading virtues. Acting against
the crowd when the signs align.

\item[\jp{随色} (\textit{zuishoku})]
``Follow-the-color''; the discretionary short-rally-fade trade of
Chapter~9.

\end{description}

\begin{editorialnote}
The editor's gloss under the header of this schedule reads:
\textit{``Same schedule as above, one and the same person.''} The
two five-stage ladders --- strong-qi accumulation and weak-qi
distribution --- are meant to be run in tandem by a single trader.
The position-size doublings (100 $\rightarrow$ 200 $\rightarrow$
400 $\rightarrow$ 800 $\rightarrow$ 1{,}000+) concentrate the
aggregate stake at the market's extremities, where the trader is
most alone against the crowd.
\end{editorialnote}
