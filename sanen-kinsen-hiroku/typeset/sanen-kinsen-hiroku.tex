\noindent\textbf{About This Edition}\\[0.3em]
\begin{sloppypar}\noindent This is the first English translation of
the \textit{Sanen Kinsen Hiroku}. The manuscript was sourced and the
translation project directed by Zach Booth. The original text is in
\textit{kobun} (classical Japanese) with \textit{kanbun kundoku}
reading marks in cursive script (\textit{kuzushiji}) on aged paper.
The translation was produced in two mechanically-separated
stages:\end{sloppypar}\par\vspace{0.4em}
\begin{sloppypar}
\noindent\textit{1. Transcription.} The manuscript images were
passed through NDL~\jp{古典籍OCR-Lite}, a specialist OCR toolchain
released by Japan's National Diet Library for Edo-period and earlier
classical texts. This step is purely mechanical: the model reads
pixels, not meaning, and cannot interpolate from context it does
not have.
\end{sloppypar}\vspace{0.3em}
\noindent\textit{2. Translation.} The OCR output was then rendered
into English with AI assistance (Claude Opus~4.6, Anthropic), working
from the mechanical transcription and consulting the manuscript images
only to sanity-check OCR artifacts.\\[0.5em]
\noindent\textbf{On Confidence.} Section headers, diagram labels,
boxed tables, and structured lists (such as the sexagenary year
cycles, the Three-Virtue verse-counts, and the four-stage method-
diagrams) are legible in the OCR and reliably translated. The dense
prose between these anchors varies: most is readable, but some
stretches --- particularly the afterword on page~32 and several
long interval rows in the body --- are fragmentary even to the
OCR. Where the underlying transcription is difficult, the
translation is marked \textit{\mbox{[Interpretive]}}. Passages so
flagged represent the reconstructed sense of the passage; they are
not the author's exact words.\\[1em]

\noindent\textbf{How to Read This Translation}\\[0.3em]
\noindent The body text of this book is a direct translation of the
source manuscript. The chapter and section headings are editorial,
added for navigation. Footnotes marked ``\textit{Translator's note}''
provide historical context, definitions, and comparative observations.
Indented passages marked ``\textit{Editor's note}'' provide brief
editorial bridges where the manuscript's structure requires
explanation. Chapter lengths vary because the translation follows the
manuscript's own proportions --- some topics receive extensive
treatment in the source, others only a line or two.\\[0.5em]
\noindent\textbf{Structure of the Text.}
Chapter~1 is the preface, grounding the book in Neo-Confucian
cosmology and naming the Three Monkeys as a contrarian discipline.
Chapters 2--3 establish the favorable/adverse year cycle and the
enumerated maxims that act on it. Chapter~4 presents the four-stage
method-diagrams (half-plank, at-ease, reversal). Chapter~5 is the
sixty-year weather almanac. Chapters 6--7 build the decision
framework: the Nine-Body Song and the forty-eight verses of the
Three Virtues. Chapters 8--11 are the quantitative core: the
World-Levels Diagram, the Strong-Qi / Weak-Qi five-stage secrets, the
compound trades, the Grand Diagram, and the three first-tier family
secrets. Chapter~12 is the editor's afterword.

\chapter*{Translator's Introduction}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Translator's Introduction}
\markboth{Translator's Introduction}{}

The \textit{Sanen Kinsen Hiroku} (\jp{三猿金泉秘録}), or ``Three Monkeys
Gold Spring Secret Record,'' is one of the most celebrated texts in the
history of Japanese market speculation.\tn{The manuscript is dated
\jp{嘉永四年五月廿日} (1851) and identifies itself as a copy of the
K\=omond\=o (\jp{好問堂}) edition, published at Igarashi-tsu
(\jp{五十嵐津}). The text references the Tenmei era (1781--1789)
extensively for its price data.}

\begin{sloppypar}
The title alludes to the proverb of the three
monkeys---``see no evil, hear no evil, speak no evil''
(\jp{見ざる、聞かざる、言わざる})---reinterpreted
as a warning to the trader: do not be swayed by what you \textit{see} others
doing, what you \textit{hear} as rumor, or what you \textit{say} in haste.
Instead, observe the deeper patterns.
\end{sloppypar}

\section*{Authorship and the Edo Rice Market}

The manuscript names its author as Ushida Gonzabur\=o (\jp{牛田権三郎}),
an Osaka D\=ojima trader from Ise province who wrote under the pen name
Jiunsai (\jp{慈雲斎}). The text was composed around 1755 and later
revised and corrected by Narukawa Takenosuke (\jp{鳴川猛之助}). It
belongs to a genre of ``secret market transmission texts''
(\jp{相場秘伝書}, \textit{s\=oba hidensho}) that circulated among
Edo-period trading houses.\tn{The inner title page of the manuscript
appears to bear a small label that may read \jp{三猿軍書選}
(\textit{Sanen gunsho sen}, ``Selection from the Three Monkeys
Military/Strategy Books''), though the label is partially obscured and
this reading is uncertain.}

During the Tokugawa period (1603--1868), rice was the basis of the economy.
The D\=ojima Rice Exchange (\jp{堂島米会所}) in Osaka functioned as a
sophisticated futures market where ``book rice'' (\jp{帳合米}) was traded
as forward contracts.\tn{The D\=ojima Rice Exchange was established in 1697
as a spot market. Organized futures trading (\jp{帳合米}) was formally
authorized by the shogunate in 1730, making it one of the world's first
organized futures markets.}

\section*{A Note on Attribution}

English-language sources almost universally attribute this text to Honma
Munehisa (\jp{本間宗久}) of Sakata, often under the title ``The Fountain
of Gold---The Three Monkey Record of Money.'' This attribution is
incorrect.

The manuscript names its author as Ushida Gonzabur\=o and its editor as
Narukawa Takenosuke---as do the National Diet Library catalog, the CiNii
bibliographic record, and every Japanese-language reference work that
discusses it. In Japanese scholarship, this is not controversial:
the NDL, CiNii, Kyoto University's digital archive, and sources from
Honma's own hometown (the Shonai Securities company of Sakata) all
attribute the \textit{Sanen Kinsen Hiroku} to Ushida.

Honma Munehisa was a real historical figure---a Sakata trader who was
expelled from his own family for speculating with company capital and who
became legendary in his own right. Texts were later attributed to him
(the \textit{S\=oky\=u-\=o Hiroku} and \textit{S\=oba Sanmai-den}),
though no Edo-period originals of these survive; Japanese scholars suspect
they were compiled in the Meiji era. Honma's texts have entirely different
titles and contents from Ushida's.

The confusion appears to have entered English through Steve Nison's
influential \textit{Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques} (1991),
a book that did enormous service in introducing Japanese technical
analysis to Western traders. In synthesizing a large body of
Japanese-language material for an English-speaking audience, Nison
consolidated several distinct figures and texts into a single
narrative---an understandable simplification given the complexity of the
source tradition. Nison himself corrected the record in
\textit{Beyond Candlesticks} (1994, p.\,14), writing that his research
indicated Honma ``likely did not use candlestick charts.'' But the first
book reached millions of readers, and the broader conflation of names
and titles took on a life of its own.

In the decades since, English sources have merged Ushida's title with
Honma's name, creating a phantom text: ``The Fountain of Gold'' (\jp{金泉})
and ``Three Monkeys'' (\jp{三猿}) are both from Ushida's title; the Honma
texts have completely different names. Two English-language books have been
published under variations of this text's title. Neither translates it.
Both render a separate body of trading maxims from the Honma/Sakata
tradition---a related but distinct lineage---while putting Ushida's title
on the cover.

What follows is, to my knowledge, the first English translation of the
actual \textit{Sanen Kinsen Hiroku}, made directly from the 1851
manuscript held at Kyoto University.

\section*{A Note on Method}

\begin{sloppypar}
The translation proceeds in two mechanically-separated stages. The
cursive manuscript pages are first read by a specialist OCR
toolchain, NDL~\jp{古典籍OCR-Lite}, released by Japan's National
Diet Library for Edo-period and earlier classical texts. The OCR is
mechanical: it reads pixels and emits characters, with no
interpretive access to the text's meaning. An LLM (Claude
Opus~4.6, Anthropic) then works from the mechanical transcription
to produce the English, consulting the manuscript images only to
sanity-check OCR artifacts.
\end{sloppypar}

The separation is deliberate. A single model asked to read cursive
script and produce English in one pass has no way to distinguish
between characters it has read and characters it has guessed,
because both are generated by the same mechanism. Splitting the
two stages makes the distinction visible: where the OCR failed,
the book says so; where the OCR returned a plausibly-wrong
character, the correction is flagged inline. Every English
sentence is traceable back to a specific line of mechanical
transcription, which is traceable back to a specific pixel-region
of the manuscript scan.

The full audit trail --- the manuscript scans, the mechanical
transcription at the character level, and the translation files
--- is published alongside this book at the URL given in the back
matter.

\chapter{The Preface: Cosmology of the Market}

\begin{center}
\jp{三猿金泉録序}\\[0.3em]
\textit{Preface to the Three Monkeys Gold Spring Record}
\end{center}

\vspace{1em}

\section{The Supreme Ultimate}

\lettrine[lines=2]{T}{he Supreme Ultimate}\tn{\jp{太極}
(\textit{taikyoku}) --- the foundational concept of Neo-Confucian
cosmology. The opening echoes Zhou Dunyi's \textit{Taijitu shuo}
(\textit{Explanation of the Diagram of the Supreme Ultimate},
mid-11th c.).} moves and begets Yang; its motion reaches its limit
and becomes still. In stillness it begets Yin; stillness reaches
its limit and moves again. Movement and stillness are the root of
one another.

The principle of one stillness and one movement is the principle
by which Heaven and Earth rise and fall. The rise and fall of rice
--- like the circuit of yin and yang --- alternates between strong-
spirited force and weak-spirited force, and each contains the seed
of its opposite. When the strong force has driven prices to their
ceiling, the weak force is already present within it; when the
weak force has driven them to their floor, the strong force is
likewise present.

When the crowd's spirit is weak, it is the principle of rice's
rising. When the crowd's spirit is strong, it is the seed of rice's
falling. All of this is the principle beyond ordinary nature
(\jp{天性理外の理}, \textit{tensei rigai no ri}).

\section{Sixty Years of Study}

For sixty years\tn{\jp{六十年来} (\textit{rokuj\=u-nen rai}). The
phrase admits both a literal autobiographical reading (``for the
past sixty years'') and a more idiomatic ``over many decades''
sense; the literal reading is taken here in keeping with the
following first-person clauses.} I have cast my mind upon this
craft, devising by day and by night, and have at length opened up
the insight into rice's strong and weak forces. I have established
the settled method of rice trading, and have compiled a single
volume of secret writings, giving it the title \textit{Three
Monkeys Gold Spring Record}.

\section{The Shape of the Rice Grain}

The form of the rice grain: rounded in the middle, pointed at top
and bottom. The round is yang; the pointed is yin.

Receiving the breath of Heaven, Earth, Yin and Yang, rice nourishes
the four classes --- warrior, farmer, artisan, merchant\tn{\jp{士農
工商} (\textit{shi-n\=o-k\=o-sh\=o}), the classical Japanese social
stratification.} --- and is the first treasure of all under Heaven.

\section{The Three Monkeys}

The Three Monkeys are: the See-Monkey, the Hear-Monkey, and the
Speak-Monkey.

When the eye perceives strong-spiritedness, do not let the heart
sink into the abyss of that strength. Hold it only within the
heart, and sell.

When the ear hears weak-spiritedness, do not let the heart sink
into the abyss of that weakness. Hold it only within the heart,
and buy.

Whether you see or hear the strong or the weak, do not speak of it
to others. Words lead hearts astray. This is the secret of the
three monkeys.

``Gold Spring Record''---this is the style of this book.

\section{The Empty Principle and the Fixed Form}

The principle of highs and lows is an empty principle
(\jp{空理}, \textit{k\=uri}): not visible to the eye, a thing whose
very body is without shade or form.

Reading the first line's sense: it is a principle by which neither
the rise nor the fall is ever fixed --- this is the empty principle.
If we take it only as empty, we will find no hour in a thousand
years to buy or to sell.

Reading the second line's sense: the formless thing does have a
body. If there is a body, there is a fixed form (\jp{定式},
\textit{j\=oshiki}); if there is a fixed form, there is a trade.

The fixed form of Buddhism is the Five Precepts. The fixed form of
Confucianism is the Five Constants. The fixed form of Shinto is the
Three Virtues of Wisdom, Benevolence, and Courage. Each has its own
fixed form.

\chapter{Favorable and Adverse Years}

In years when old rice is plentiful and its low price transfers to
the new rice, there is a rising force of markets. This is called a
favorable year (\jp{順来}, \textit{junrai}).

In years when rice is scarce and the high price transfers to the
new harvest, there is a downward force. Even if a strong rally
appears in the sixth, seventh or eighth month, the year is one of
downward force nonetheless. This is called an adverse year
(\jp{逢来}, \textit{airai}).

\section{The Twelve-to-Thirteen Year Cycle}

The way of following favorable and adverse conditions: within a
period of twelve or thirteen years, the favorable years run to
roughly ten and the adverse to three. The settled form of trading
divides into two, and comprises: the Twelve-Cycle Trading of
favorable and adverse years; the Family-Tradition Mirror of Highs
and Lows; the Thirty-Eight Articles of Trading; the Fifteen
Prohibitions; and the Annual Record of Fortune-Qi and Plenty-and-
Want.

Studied through, this is the wondrous art that does not lose once
in a thousand attempts. Truly, the secret treasure of the family.
Keep it secret, keep it secret.

\section{Twelve-Cycle Trading of Favorable and Adverse Years}

\begin{center}
\jp{○順来逢来十二平商内　〔天明本〕}\tn{The margin gloss
\jp{天明本} (\textit{Tenmei-bon}), ``Tenmei recension,'' recurs
throughout the book and signals a parallel reading from a Tenmei-
era (1781--1789) version of the text.}
\end{center}

\subsection*{Favorable year --- six verses on autumn-and-winter highs and lows}

Autumn cheap; an upward force; one and a half steps; the eleventh
month falls; the year-end rises.

Spring breaks; the fourth and fifth months go down; the rice of the
sixth month (Minazuki) and the seventh (Fumizuki) goes down.

\subsection*{Favorable year --- six verses on the trading method}

In autumn, buy; on the upward thrust, sell out and reverse; turn
again on the auspicious month; the year-end is at ease.

In spring, reverse; in the fourth and fifth months, buy; at the end
of the fifth month, sow this seed; settle by the end of the sixth,
seventh, and eighth months.

\subsection*{Adverse year --- six verses on autumn-and-winter highs and lows}

Autumn high; downward force; one and a half steps; the eleventh
month is cheap; even at year-end it does not rise.

\subsection*{Adverse year --- six verses on the trading method}

In autumn, the standing crop falls; on the downward push, buy a
half-plank position; on the eleventh month's mid-festival, rest
through year-end.

In spring, the market gives way at the fourth month; at the end of
the fifth, sow the buying seed; settle by the end of the sixth,
seventh, and eighth months.

\section{Family Tradition: Mirror of the Four Seasons}

\begin{center}
\jp{○家伝四季高安鑑　第二天明本}
\end{center}

\subsection*{Autumn and winter in a favorable year}

When old rice is plentiful and you read the year as abundant, know
the cheap rice as a year of upward force.

\subsection*{Autumn and winter in an adverse year}

When old rice is scarce and strong-spiritedness pushes out, know
the high rice as the harbinger of a downward year.

\subsection*{Spring in favorable and adverse years alike}

When you read it as a green rise and buy, the rice from the upper
storehouse will not easily turn cheap. When you read it as a
spring decline, the cheap winter rice will not easily settle low
in spring either.

\subsection*{Late fifth month, the pivot}

Fifth-month rice: sentiment is weak, yet the price rises. To the
children and the children's children: selling is forbidden.

\chapter{The Thirty-Eight Articles\\ and the Fifteen Prohibitions}

\section{The Thirty-Eight Articles of Trading}\tn{%
\jp{三十八ヶ条商内}:
\jt{商内}{akinai}, glossed with furigana in the manuscript
as the native Japanese reading rather than the Sino-Japanese
\textit{sh\=onai}. Each article opens with \jp{一} (``one'') and
ends in a short trading rule.}

\subsection*{Year-reads and the spread signal}

A favorable year: a downward force followed by a rise. At the
harvest moon of autumn, sow the seed of buying.

An adverse year: a downward force followed by a further fall. At
the harvest moon of autumn, sow the seed of selling.

When the spread flips from below-spread to above-spread, after
the downward thrust comes the rise --- close out your position on
the gain.

When the spread flips from above to below, after the downward
thrust comes the further decline --- close out your position on
the gain.

\subsection*{The two-hundred-tenth day}

The year when strong-spiritedness emerges is set: select it, and
on the two-hundred-tenth day\tn{\jp{二百十日}
(\textit{nihyaku-t\=oka}). Counted from the lunar-year start, it
falls in early September; the text uses it as a fixed seasonal
pivot for buying.} sow the seed of buying.

A monk says: study the \textit{Annual Record of Fortune-Qi and
Plenty-and-Want}; cultivate the strong-spirited stance; on the
two-hundred-tenth day, sow the seed of buying. This is the sense
of the verse.

\subsection*{On public sentiment}

The ear catching strong-spiritedness in the public voice is itself
the seed of rice's coming fall.

When weak-spiritedness has emerged into the open, everyone turns
strong-spirited without judgment --- sow the seed of selling.

The year in which weak-spiritedness fails to emerge into the world
is one in which rice ought to rise.

\subsection*{The contrarian maxims}

If a rice whose climbing pace is plainly visible is in front of
you, halt --- stay your strong spirit and sell.

The man who is forever weak-spirited, knowing nothing of loss or
gain --- call him a great timid one, a parishioner of the poverty
god.

\subsection*{The rhythm of the year}

The great rule: late in the fifth month is the appointed time of
selling; the harvest moon of autumn is the ripening of buying.

If the year accords with what the multitude expects, better to set
out for an unfrequented path.

To buy your rice in a single stroke is rashness. Buy in two; sell
in two.

Across a hundred \textit{tsubo} of land, ninety-nine years of
highs and lows do not exceed three steps --- take this for fact.

Even by a single move, when the step has risen, it is nine-myriad
one \textit{bu}; know it as the selling season at the celestial
origin\tn{\jp{天元} (\textit{tengen}): the ``celestial origin''
recurs as the top stage-marker of every position ladder in the
book.} of a thousand.

\section{The Fifteen Prohibitions (\jp{十五ヶ条禁制})}

When a trade is without its proper attendant, then whether or not
any gain or loss may be, trading is forbidden.

An abundant harvest: the multitude grows weak-spirited; I too grow
weak. Coming into autumn, selling is forbidden.

A lean harvest: the minor-spirit is strong, and stronger again;
when rice is plentiful, buying is forbidden.

When the rising step is so visible that even the rice itself seems
to say so, though the year is strong, selling in the fourth month
is forbidden.

Fifth-month rice: sentiment weak, yet the price rises. Until the
strength is clearly in, selling is forbidden.

On great floods or great winds sending rice spiking upward --- in
any year, buying is forbidden.

\chapter{The Method Diagrams:\\ Half-Plank, At-Ease, Reversal}

\begin{editorialnote}
The three method-diagrams are each a four-stage \textit{sequential
cycle} --- not a branching decision tree. Each specifies the fixed
sequence of four actions a trader executes as the market moves
through one complete up-and-down cycle. The differences between
them are in the size-relationship between the buy and sell legs,
which determines whether the cycle leaves the trader net long
(half-plank), net flat (at-ease), or net short (reversal).
\end{editorialnote}

\section{The Autumn-Moon Eight-Hundred-Ry\=o Ladder}

\begin{center}
\jp{大黒天神秋名月商内并二百十日商}\\[0.3em]
\textit{The Daikoku-deity's autumn-harvest-moon trade, together
with the two-hundred-tenth-day trade.}\\[0.4em]
\jp{買桶三平二来半板安楽商内}\\[0.3em]
\textit{Using the buy-bucket, three-flats-two-comings-half-plank-
at-ease method.}
\end{center}

The ladder builds an 800-\textit{ry\=o} position across six named
stages: \jt{天元}{tengen} (the celestial-origin base reference),
\jt{一乗}{ichij\=o}, \jt{二来}{nirai}, \jt{三平}{sanpei},
\jt{半板}{hanban}, and \jt{安楽}{anraku} (at ease).

Summed: an 800-\textit{ry\=o} buy, seventy \textit{tawara} of
accumulated rice, approximately thirty \textit{ry\=o} in payment,
forty \textit{ry\=o} in deposit --- altogether seventy
\textit{ry\=o}.\tn{The ladder runs as a tight column of small-hand
characters at very low OCR confidence; the 800-\textit{ry\=o}
total is supported by multiple summed-row readings, but the
70/30/40/70 cell breakdown should be treated as approximate
pending image audit.}

In the harvest-moon trade, one ought to use this diagram as the
square-rule and trade by it. When reversed, it becomes an
800-\textit{ry\=o} sell. In the trade before the two-hundred-tenth
day, likewise, one ought to trade by this diagram as the
square-rule.

A sister ladder on the following leaf, dedicated to Nishinomiya
Ebisu and Sumiyoshi Daimy\=oj\=o, runs a 600-\textit{ry\=o}
variant with a 30-\textit{tawara} bucket subtraction; another runs
a 400-\textit{ry\=o} trade with a reversal at the pivot. The same
six stage-names recur in all three.

\section{On the Great and Small Tides (\jp{大潮小汐の考})}

ships to sea. If by some chance the calm fails, it is an exception
--- know that even such an exception is only one year in a hundred.

\section{Benevolence (\jp{仁}) --- Twenty-Eight Verses}

Neither rushing the trade nor answering to impatience --- to wait
until the riding-together of waiting and gaining --- this too is
benevolence.

When a price exists such that the ten-thousand to a man have given

The root source of the favorable-virtue wisdom, in the end, is the

\subsection*{Applying the method}

Using the three-level method, one accumulates the good-and-bad of
each year's world. A year of the upper division may run on for
thirty years; a year of the lower division can run on for eight or
nine.

Consider the out-turn of the low ranking. In a middle-division
world, if the year's estimate is seven-tenths, multiply three times
seven --- twenty-one \textit{tawara}. Take this as the middle-ink
price.

For such a price: add six \textit{tawara} of the thirty-percent
drawdown buffer to get twenty-six \textit{tawara} as the middle
law. At year-end, this holds without variance --- not even one
hundredth-of-a-\textit{monme} off.

\chapter{The Compound Trades\\ and the Seven Fortunes}

\begin{center}
\jp{○七福即生秘密巻目}\\[0.3em]
\textit{The Secret of the Immediate Arising of the Seven Fortunes}
\end{center}

In general, the Tang-edge is said to be three-step-down or
three-step-up. However, as long as the buying side shows a
one-step strength, there is no losing the buying side. In any year,
take the new-rice opening price as middle-ink, and buy.

\section{The Strong-Qi Reverse-Weight Trade}

The reverse-weight trading force has its middle-ink. Up through
two steps there is no one-step; beyond two steps there is no
five-\textit{bu}.

The trader builds a long across five scaled entries, reaching a
\textbf{1{,}600-\textit{ry\=o}} total at the extreme:

\begin{quote}
Cumulative: 1{,}600-\textit{ry\=o} long held; 180 \textit{tawara}
drawdown. A ten-percent rally yields 320 \textit{tawara} gain ---
the voice of favorable virtue.
\end{quote}

\section{Favorable-Virtue Reverse-Loss Variants}

\begin{description}
\item[\jp{強気一割あげ順徳逆損商内}] Strong-qi ten-percent-up
favorable-virtue reverse-loss. Five-way graduated scale-out
(\jp{五方ならし}) across a rally.

\item[\jp{強気一割半あげ順徳逆損商内}] Same schedule keyed to a
15\,\% rally threshold, in a tighter \textit{tawara} band. Net:
800-\textit{ry\=o} sell; 70-\textit{tawara} drawdown at the turn;
160-\textit{tawara} profit per one-step decline.

\item[\jp{強気二刻あげ順徳丞様商内}] Same idiom keyed to a two-step
rally. At the \textit{tengen}-mark on the second pass, the position
reverses, and a further 200-\textit{ry\=o} sell is initiated.
\end{description}

\section{The Random-Edge Trade (\jp{度端一六の商内})}

From a 20-\textit{tawara} middle-ink, whenever the market slumps
beyond three steps --- or, alternately, rallies beyond three
steps --- always trade the one-hundred-\textit{ry\=o}-sized lot at
the low level.

Even if thinking in terms of one thousand or two thousand
\textit{ry\=o}, pack it in lightly. Even a thirty-percent-plus
drawdown is only one case in a thousand; even a thirty-percent-
plus rally is only a mountain-of-one in a thousand.

\section{The Stake-Use Trade (\jp{立用商内})}

In the stake-use trade, you do not speak of the gap --- rather,
from having heard the future ups and downs of rice, you reflect
carefully and stake accordingly.

The stake-use is not much of a wrapping-in for those who trade in
large size: the reason being that when you hold a long of 50{,}000
or 100{,}000 \textit{ry\=o}, even if you wish to sell, as soon as
you do, the side-market chases you down six or seven \textit{bu}
and can pursue you for up to a month.

\section{The Follow-the-Color Trade (\jp{随色商内})}

The law of the follow-the-color trade: at those moments when the
genuine awareness is dulled, when nothing exceptional has
occurred, and one is inclined to say no imperial edict will come,
rice suddenly rallies five \textit{bu} or seven \textit{bu}. Have
that ship sold, and buy at the place where it retraces five,
seven, seven. This is called \textit{follow-the-color}.

Still, stake only a hundred or two hundred \textit{ry\=o}. Without
certainty, to overdo the ease is the bold overhand of luggage.\tn{The
figurative \jp{手荷の強気} is opaque in isolation; likely something
like ``the trader's own baggage'' --- a warning that an unsupported
show of confidence is the trader's self-weight, not the market's.}

\chapter{Strong Qi, Weak Qi,\\ and the Three First-Tier Secrets}

\section{The Bullish Bias}

On the buying side: the principles of drought, wind-and-rain,
heavenly disaster, and buying-enclosure --- across spring, summer,
autumn, and winter. Many are the reasons for strong spirit among
those who take these as their ground.

On the selling side: there is not one principle other than waiting
for the buying-enclosure to collapse. It is for this reason that,
when the diagram is laid out, there is a one-tenth strength bias
on the buying side.

\section{The Effect of Strong Qi (\jp{強気の功})}

When wind and rain are violent, there are ship-wrecks in every
season; Edo and Osaka alike have gaps in shipping. The first
principle is that each person takes heed. At times of violent
wind, know it as a critical matter across all four seasons.

\subsection*{Monthly weather almanac}

From the third or fourth month through the eighth or ninth month:
when wind-and-rain are violent, the effect is flood damage to
fields.

Fourth or fifth month: drought means no planting. Long rains rot
the barley.

Fourth or fifth month: a great wind strikes the barley.

Sixth or seventh month: a great wind strikes the miscellaneous
grains generally. Drought strikes field crops.

Ninth month: a wind strikes the rice directly.

Eighth or ninth month: summer rain rots the glutinous rice.

Fifth or sixth month: long rains bring cold that can extend half
the year into a damaging autumn.

\section{The Effect of Weak Qi (\jp{弱気の功})}

At the very ear of a great-high price,\tn{The idiom \jp{大高直段
の耳々} --- literally ``at the ear(s) of the great-high price'' ---
means at its furthest extremity or peak.} there will be proclamations
on sake and the like --- imperial notices. Besides these, there
will also be proclamations of town-inspections. Consider these
too.

Or --- a wind that did not blow is said to have blown; a minor
damage is spoken of as though it were the full ten-tenths; and
there are years when feigned buy-ins are performed. Japan is the
country of the gods: a country that takes uprightness as its root,
and so the natural heaven is present above it.

\section{The Rarity of the Perfect Year}

Without damage at the planting, without the heavenly disaster of
drought or wind-and-rain --- what one may call a world at the full
ten-tenths cannot come to be more than three years in a hundred.

Even if our own country is abundant, there may be countries to
east, west, south, or north that have been blown over. Do not, do
not, assume from one view of a single abundant-peace-year that the
whole of Japan will be likewise.

In general, it is from the cheapest country first that prices
rise. Consider this.

\section{The Three First-Tier Secrets}

Every year, read the opening trading-day level as the middle-ink:
this is the first secret.

Buy the middle-ink at once: this is the second.

At the outset, stake the hundred-\textit{ry\=o} trade-seal in
true gold of one hundred \textit{ry\=o}: this is the third
secret.

\section{Yin-Rice and Yang-Rice (\jp{陰米陽米})}

There are such things as yin-rice and yang-rice. The rice that
rises is yang; the rice that falls is yin. The reason: yin rolls
into yang; yang climbs directly upward.

\chapter*{Glossary of Key Terms}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Glossary of Key Terms}
\markboth{Glossary of Key Terms}{}

\begin{description}[style=nextline, leftmargin=2em, font=\normalfont\bfseries]

\item[\jp{逢来} (\textit{airai})]
Adverse conditions; a period of generally falling prices, typically
lasting roughly three years within a twelve-to-thirteen-year cycle.

\item[\jp{安楽} (\textit{anraku})]
``At ease''; the second of the four-stage method diagrams
(Chapter~4). A net-flat cycle of four alternating
100-\textit{ry\=o} buys and sells --- so called because the trader
carries no overnight directional exposure.

\item[\jp{智} (\textit{chi})]
Wisdom; the first of the \textit{three} trading virtues
(智仁勇). Seeing the pattern, knowing the season.

\item[\jp{帳合米} (\textit{ch\=oaimai})]
``Book rice''; forward contracts on rice, as opposed to physical
rice.

\item[\jp{中墨} (\textit{nakasumi} / \textit{ch\=uboku})]
``Middle-ink''; the three-starred center-cell of the World-Levels
Diagram (Chapter~8); the reference price against which the
five-stage strong-qi and weak-qi schedules are read. Taken each
year as the new-rice opening price.

\item[\jp{度端} (\textit{dohan})]
``Random-edge''; the family of discretionary scaling trades
presented on page~28 under the heading \jp{度端極意の巻} (``The
Secret of the Random-Edge'').

\item[\jp{堂島} (\textit{D\=ojima})]
The D\=ojima Rice Exchange in Osaka; the central marketplace for
rice futures trading during the Edo period.

\item[\jp{半板} (\textit{hanban})]
``Half-plank''; the first of the four-stage method diagrams
(Chapter~4). A net-long cycle of 1000-\textit{ry\=o} buys
alternating with 500-\textit{ry\=o} sells --- the trader is always
running with half his plank still underfoot.

\item[\jp{穂足} (\textit{hosoku})]
``Spike-foot,'' or the ear-of-grain figure; the foundational
candle-pattern metaphor introduced in the preface, from the shape
of the rice grain itself (round in the middle, pointed at top and
bottom).

\item[\jp{仁} (\textit{jin})]
Benevolence; the second of the three trading virtues, and the
largest of the three verse-ranks (twenty-eight of the forty-eight
verses). Patience, waiting, not rushing.

\item[\jp{人気} (\textit{jinki})]
``People-qi''; market sentiment; the collective mood of traders.

\item[\jp{慈雲斎} (\textit{Jiunsai})]
Ushida Gonzabur\=o's pen name; appears once in the manuscript
immediately above his given name (page~5), attached to the text's
original composition in H\=oreki~5 (1755).

\item[\jp{順来} (\textit{junrai})]
Favorable conditions; a period of generally rising prices,
typically lasting roughly ten years in a twelve-to-thirteen-year
cycle.

\item[\jp{順徳} (\textit{juntoku})]
``Favorable virtue''; the positive-expectancy direction of the
compound reverse-loss trades of Chapter~9.

\item[\jp{禁制} (\textit{kinsei})]
``Prohibition''; the closing clause-word of each of the Fifteen
Prohibitions (Chapter~3).

\item[\jp{古米} (\textit{komai})]
Old rice; rice from previous harvests.

\item[\jp{空理} (\textit{k\=uri})]
``Empty principle''; the preface's term for the inherent
formlessness of market motion. Paired with \jp{定式}
(\textit{j\=oshiki}, ``fixed form'') in the philosophical move that
justifies the whole project: the motion is empty, yet has a form,
and from the form a method can be derived.

\item[\jp{両} (\textit{ry\=o})]
The gold coin unit of the Edo period; the standard unit of capital.

\item[\jp{新米} (\textit{shinmai})]
New rice; freshly harvested rice. The opening trading-day price of
new rice is taken as the \textit{nakasumi} each year.

\item[\jp{底} (\textit{soko})]
Bottom or floor; the lowest point of the price range.

\item[\jp{太極} (\textit{taikyoku})]
Supreme Ultimate; the Neo-Confucian concept representing
undifferentiated unity. Opens the preface; named in the Nine-Body
Song (Chapter~6).

\item[\jp{俵} (\textit{tawara} / \textit{hy\=o})]
A bale of rice; the standard unit of measure and price quotation.

\item[\jp{天元} (\textit{tengen})]
``Celestial origin''; the top stage-marker of every position
ladder in the book --- the baseline price around which
accumulation and distribution ladders are calibrated.

\item[\jp{天井} (\textit{tenj\=o})]
Ceiling; the top of the price range on the World-Levels Diagram.

\item[\jp{転変} (\textit{tenpen})]
``Reversal''; the third of the four-stage method diagrams
(Chapter~4). A net-short cycle of 1000-\textit{ry\=o} buys
alternating with 2000-\textit{ry\=o} sells --- each sell
simultaneously unwinds the prior buy and opens an equal-size short.

\item[\jp{天明本} (\textit{Tenmei-bon})]
``Tenmei recension''; the margin-annotation marker that recurs
throughout the book indicating a parallel reading from a Tenmei-
era (1781--1789) version of the text.

\item[\jp{強気} (\textit{ky\=oki})]
Strong qi; bullish sentiment or upward force. One of the two
central force-concepts of the book.

\item[\jp{運気} (\textit{unki})]
Fortune-qi; the prevailing momentum of a given period. Compiled as
an almanac in Chapter~5, the \jp{万歳運気豊凶録}.

\item[\jp{弱気} (\textit{jakki} / \textit{yowaki})]
Weak qi; bearish sentiment or downward force.

\item[\jp{勇} (\textit{y\=u})]
Courage; the third of the three trading virtues. Acting against
the crowd when the signs align.

\item[\jp{随色} (\textit{zuishoku})]
``Follow-the-color''; the discretionary short-rally-fade trade of
Chapter~9.

\end{description}
