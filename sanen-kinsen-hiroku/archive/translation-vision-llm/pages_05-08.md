# Pages 5–8: Favorable/Adverse Conditions, Price Cycles, and Candlestick Records

## Page 5 — Favorable Conditions (*Junrai*) continued; Opening of the Secret Record

The text continues describing the seasonal rice trading pattern:

During favorable-condition years, one expects prosperity for roughly twelve to thirteen years. In such periods, rice generally trends high. When old rice (*komai*) is in a year of abundance, it will be cheap. One should buy around the fourth month; by mid-sixth month prices will be high—this is the selling point.

In autumn, buy again around the seventh month. Toward year-end, old rice prices rise and one should sell. This is the favorable-year pattern.

For adverse-condition years (*airai*), the cheap period for old rice falls around the fourth through sixth months. In autumn, one should buy, and by year-end the rice price climbs to a high.

**Family tradition handed down by Ushida Gonzaburō.**

## Page 6 — Favorable and Adverse Years, Seasonal Patterns

### ◯ On Favorable Conditions for the Four Seasons, High and Low Prices — A Song (*uta*)

*In a year of favorable conditions, the autumn harvest is plentiful; the highs and lows can be estimated for four seasons.*

*In spring, from around the fourth month onward, buy at the fifth month level; by the end of the year, the high price will come.*

### Favorable and Adverse Conditions in a Cycle

In favorable-condition periods (*junrai*), the rice market's people-sentiment (*jinki*) for each month can be read directly; the five-month and year-end levels become the basis.

In a great flood year (*ōmizu*), the rice market [reacts] to the twelfth month. The monthly sentiment varies, and the autumn rice trades at its own level.

## Page 7 — Thirty-Eight Seasonal Trading Rules

### ◯ Thirty-Eight Provisions for Seasonal Trading (*Sanjūhachi-kajō*)

In a year of adverse conditions (*airai*), from around the fifth month onward, summer rice drops and autumn months see further decline. Autumn months are for buying; favorable months are for the pattern of waiting.

**On the grades of harvest quality:**
- **Good harvest** (*jōsaku* 上作): rice is abundant; prices are depressed accordingly
- **Average harvest** (*chūsaku* 中作): yields fall between good and poor estimates
- **Poor harvest** (*gesaku* 下作): rice is scarce; prices rise accordingly

*[Note: 上作/中作/下作 refer to harvest quality grades, not geographic regions.]*

For the months from the tenth onward, one should consider the first ten days of each month; the rice market's movement, whether up or down, can be read. The strong and the weak patterns alternate. The key is to examine the movement within each four-to-five month period.

In the fifth month, in a year of poor harvest, the rice situation becomes tight; one should buy at the lower level. But in a strong-yield (*tsuyoki*) period of four years, the rice pattern reverses.

### Weak Trends and the World's Conditions (*Jakusei*)

When weak qi pervades the world (*yowaki sese*), and things seem to continue weakening, the rice price is at a level that appears flat—this is the year of rice buying.

## Page 8 — Candlestick Records (*Hōsoku no Roku*) and Trade Calculations

### 穂足の録 — *Record of Spike/Candle Patterns*

When buying and holding, one must consider the fortune (*un*) and the momentum (*ki*). The transport and supply dynamics, whether two hundred days or three hundred days, the seasonal movements and annual patterns must be compared.

One should not be impatient: observe for two or three days, then when the rice begins to move, buy. Those who buy impulsively and hold stubbornly will fail. Gold and rice are like flowing water; one must read them well, buy carefully, and sell at the right moment.

**Trading with 100 Ryō:**

In the Tenmei era (*Tenmei nenchū*, 1781–1789), trading within the market:
- With capital of 100 ryō, buying at various price levels (*dan/tawara*) across multiple positions
- Entries at 20 *tawara* (bales), adjusting at each stage
- Building positions across four months: buying and selling to compound

**Diagram notations show a systematic position-building method:**
- Initial buy, then subsequent adds at lower levels
- Final position calculated across 17–18 stages
- Running total reaching 200+ ryō through methodical trading of 500–800 bale positions
