# Pages 17–20: The Eight Rules, Market Types, and the Virtue of Benevolence

## Page 17 — The Eight Rules of Market Reading

### ◯ 八極 三千目 — *Eight Poles / Three Thousand Points*

The eight extremes of market behavior, observable across all conditions:

**◯ ◯ ◯ ◯** (four marked principles on the right page):

1. When prices have fallen for five months, from around the third month onward, observe carefully. When rice reaches a bottom and the downward pressure exhausts itself, this is recognized by the absence of further selling despite bad news.

2. In the eighth month, the rice of the new harvest approaches; the old rice's price must be read in relation to the harvest forecast. Compare this year's situation with the previous year's.

3. Devotion to one's own view—holding it exclusively—leads to ruin. One must listen to all reports and weigh them, yet act on one's own seasoned judgment. This is the way.

### ◯ 一旅将壱九之勢 — *The Force of One-Nine / The General's Single Campaign*

The analogy of a military general: just as a general must commit his forces decisively at the right moment, the trader must commit capital when the signs align. Hesitation at the critical moment means the opportunity passes.

The categories of market force:
- **陽** (*Yō*) — Yang / Bullish force
- **陰** (*In*) — Yin / Bearish force
- **太極** (*Taikyoku*) — Supreme Ultimate / Equilibrium
- **陰合** (*Ingō*) — Yin convergence

When ten thousand people (*mannin*) are all of one mind—all buying or all selling—the reversal is near. A thousand people of the same opinion is a strong signal; ten thousand unanimous is extreme. At such moments, the solitary trader who goes against them has the advantage.

**High and Low (*Takayasu*):** The great floods of certain months, the fifth month insect damage—these drive short-term surges. But the underlying seasonal pattern must be followed.

## Page 18 — Price Determination and Trading Intelligence

### 天怪 — *Celestial Anomalies*

When extraordinary signs appear—unusual weather in certain months, or disruptions to the normal seasonal flow—the trader must first determine whether these are temporary or lasting in their effect.

The standard rice price across months: the ordinary level (*futsū*) is established over a year, and deviations from this level are traded.

### ◯ 買付を揚年名月茶まで平定式 — *Standard Method for Buying and Holding to the Target Month*

**平楽定式** — *Standard of Calm Profit:*

Buying is done at a set price level; one then holds until the target selling month. This requires patience: one must not sell early when prices dip temporarily, nor hold past the target when greed urges more.

### ◯ 智 — *Wisdom (Chi)* — Ten Principles

Wisdom (*chi*) in trading has ten aspects:
- Knowing when to enter
- Knowing when to exit
- Reading the mood of the market
- Understanding seasonal patterns
- Patience in waiting for the right moment

The text discusses the relationship between different regional markets (*Dōjima*, *Bakan*, etc.) and how information flows between them. The timing gap between regional price movements creates opportunities for the informed trader.

**On Favorable Conditions (*Junrai*):** When favorable conditions turn and the market reverses, the signs are subtle at first. The price may stop rising for several days; then a slight decline sets in. Those who recognize this early transition profit the most.

## Page 19 — Benevolence (*Jin*): The First Virtue of Trading

### ◯ 仁 — *Benevolence (Jin)* — 壱首 · 壱百五拾目 (First Article; the 150th Item)

**壱買段せ、其まゝ本へもどる** — *"Buy once, and it returns to its original level."*

When one buys and the price immediately returns to the purchase level, one should wait. This is the test of benevolence—patience and calm in the face of an unmoving market. One must not panic and sell at cost; the market is testing the trader's resolve.

If after buying, the price falls one hundred *mon* and then recovers to within one hundred of the purchase price, this pattern—repeated over three years—teaches the trader to endure. In each of those years, the rice that was bought at what seemed a bad time eventually proved profitable for those who held.

**On Upper, Middle, and Lower Positions:**

- **上位 (*jōi*)** — Upper level: when rice has risen strongly and is at the top of its range
- **中位 (*chūi*)** — Middle level: rice is at its ordinary seasonal level
- **下位 (*gei*)** — Lower level: rice has fallen and is at the bottom of its range

At the upper level, one should be selling, not buying. At the lower level, buying. At the middle level, one should wait and observe which direction momentum carries the price. Many traders err by buying at the upper level (chasing) or selling at the lower level (panicking).

When the price has been at a low level for three hundred days or more, and a rise begins, and then the price returns and falls again to a new low—this is the final bottom (*soko*). Buy here.

## Page 20 — The Six Observations; Proportional Trading

### The Six Secret Observations (*Roku Hiken*)

When watching the rice market, one must first observe these six things:
1. **二割** (*ni-wari*) — the 20% level: if rice falls 20% from its high, this is a meaningful correction
2. **三割** (*san-wari*) — 30%: a major correction inviting careful buying
3. **高の内** (*taka no uchi*) — within the high range: movements here are distribution
4. **横の平** (*yoko no hira*) — sideways movement: the market is undecided
5. **人の気** (*hito no ki*) — people's sentiment: the crowd's mood
6. **大勢** (*taisei*) — the grand trend: the overarching direction

The rice market's six initial observations determine all trading. When a person wishes to buy, he should first check whether the price has already risen 20–30% from its base. If it has, the opportunity for buying has passed; one must wait for the next decline.

**On Proportional Entry (*Bunsan*):**

All buying and selling should be done proportionally. When buying, one should think of mountains—rice is like mountains and water; it naturally rises and falls. The great mountain does not rise in a day, and neither does the great price movement.

One should buy in portions: first a small amount, then add as the direction confirms. Never commit the full position at once. Similarly, selling should be staged.

**千人の気** — *The Sentiment of a Thousand People:*

When a thousand people are of one mind regarding the market, and a single trader perceives something different, he must weigh his own observation against the crowd's. If his reasoning is sound and based on the six observations, he may be right even against one thousand.

Rice buying and selling should be approached with deliberation, as one approaches a great mountain.
